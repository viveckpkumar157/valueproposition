The Problem

Feature-first product thinking is the enemy.

The product industry has built a set of reflexes that feel productive and produce failure.

What most teams do
"Let's run a user survey." (Surveys confirm what teams already believe.)
"NPS is down. Ship more features." (NPS does not tell you what to build.)
"We need better retention mechanics." (Mechanics on top of wrong P.)
"Our activation rate is low. Improve onboarding." (Onboarding removes wrong barrier.)
"Users engage daily but churn at Month 3." (Metric Features confused for value.)
What the framework does instead
Finds the workaround. The bright-spot customer who already solved the problem. No survey needed.
Names the life change, not the feature. P at outcome level, not feature level.
Identifies the specific lock-in type before designing liberation.
Traces every feature to the obstacle thesis in two steps. Cuts what cannot be traced.
Diagnoses whether the moat exists and whether the customer can feel it.

"Kingfisher had a tagline. IndiGo had a system. One competed on perception. The other competed on delivery. Only one is still flying."

The Framework

Two parts. Eight questions. One system.

BELT diagnoses the problem before any design decision is made. POLE engineers four distinct value propositions, each doing a different job at a different moment.

Part One: BELT - Diagnosis

B
Behaviour
What are they doing despite friction?
Find the workaround. Score FUDU. Gate: is this uniquely underserved?
E
Enduring
What outcome do they actually need?
Life Change level, not Feature level. Life Change level. Use the So What? technique.
L
Lock-in
Why have they not switched?
Switching Cost / Search Cost / Inertia. Each requires different liberation.
T
Type
Transient or Enabling?
Does the 100th use deliver more value than the first? If no: Transient. Do not build E architecture on it.

Part Two: POLE - Design

P
The Destination
The 12-month life change.
Ongoing / Terminal / Intermittent. HOW Audit before writing.
O
The Belief Moment
Breaks scepticism in session 1.
Understandable before first use. Verifiable after. Traces to the obstacle thesis.
L
Liberation
Removes the specific barrier.
Traces to the BELT L diagnosis. Four O+L types. Aim for convergence.
E
Retention Arc
Makes leaving costly.
E-Delivered per session. E-Accrued across sessions — the irreplaceable moat.
The key principle
Your product does not have one value proposition. It has four. Each does a different job.

See it in action

MyFitnessPal: a 15-minute teardown

200 million users. A feature used daily. And a gap that Noom turned into a $600 million business. Here is what the framework reveals.

How it is currently designed
P
"Track your calories." Feature level. The mechanism, not the outcome.
O
Calorie goal set in onboarding. O fires for logging, not for understanding.
L
Requires calorie target and weight goal before any value is felt. Barrier is Inertia — exactly the wrong design.
E
Streak badge, daily reminder, barcode scanner. All optimise logging. None produce the outcome the user came for.
How it should be designed
P
"For adults who have tried and failed through willpower alone, MFP delivers insight into how food affects how you feel - so tracking becomes unnecessary as awareness becomes habit."
O
Day 7: "You consistently eat 400 more calories on days you skip breakfast." First insight only this product could produce. That is O. Not a calorie count.
L
"Log one meal. No calorie target required." Zero-commitment first step. The act of logging is simultaneously the first data point toward the insight. Type 1 Convergence.
E
Relevance is load-bearing: insights must be specific to this person. "You have identified 5 patterns about your body no other app has." That is the moat. That is the referral.
The commercial gap this reveals
Noom charged $60/month for the insight layer MFP left on the table. One sentence change to P. Everything downstream follows. Noom built a $600 million business on the gap.
