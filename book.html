Foreword
The Trap Nobody Talks About
You are probably solving the wrong type of problem

There is a specific way product teams fail that nobody in the industry talks about clearly. They solve the wrong type of problem. Not a bad problem. Not a fake problem. The wrong type. And the distinction is everything.

A transient problem goes away on its own or after first use. The customer needed something once. You delivered it. They left. That is not a product failure. That is the natural consequence of solving a transient problem. But most product teams treat that churn as a retention problem and spend years trying to fix it with features, notifications, and loyalty mechanics. None of it works. You cannot retain someone who never had a reason to stay.

An enduring problem persists. It recurs. The customer needs a solution every week, every month, for years. This is the only type of problem that can sustain a product. And it is rarer than most teams think.

"If your product is solving a transient problem, you do not have a retention problem. You have a diagnosis problem. No feature will fix it."

This book is a system for getting that diagnosis right before you design anything. It has two parts. BELT is the diagnostic engine: four questions that force you to classify the problem type before any design decision is made. POLE is the design architecture: four value propositions, each doing a different job at a different moment in the customer journey.

The sequence is non-negotiable. BELT before POLE. Diagnosis before design. The quality of everything you build in POLE is entirely determined by the quality of your BELT diagnosis. Skip the diagnosis and you design confidently toward the wrong destination.

The framework is not gentle. It will surface the gap between what your team believes about the product and what customers actually experience. That gap is usually larger and more structural than anyone expects. It is almost never fixed by a new feature.

Before you read further
Ask one question about your current product: is the problem it solves transient or enduring? If you cannot answer with confidence, that uncertainty is the most important thing this book will resolve.
How to Read This Book
Do not skip BELT to get to POLE. The sequence is the point. The quality of POLE design is entirely determined by the quality of BELT diagnosis.
The Introduction chapter on utility vs entertainment products is not optional context. It is the first gate. Read it before running any diagnostic.
Case studies in Part Six are fully worked. Read at least one before running the framework on your own product. They calibrate what the framework actually reveals, not just what it describes.
The 30-minute application guide at the end is where this becomes operational. Every concept in every chapter points toward that guide.
Introduction
Where This Framework Applies
Utility products, entertainment products, and the difference that determines everything

Before running any BELT diagnostic, answer one question: is this a utility product or an entertainment product? The answer determines whether the framework applies, how it applies, and where its limits are.

The Core Distinction

A utility product solves a specific, recurring problem. The customer uses it because it moves them toward a defined outcome they would pursue even without the product. The product is instrumental. Value is measured in progress.

An entertainment product delivers hedonic value. The customer uses it because the experience itself is enjoyable. There is no destination. There is no problem being solved. Value is measured in engagement, not progress.

The test
Ask a loyal customer: "What specific outcome does this product help you achieve?" A utility customer can answer this. An entertainment customer gives you consumption language: "It's entertaining," "I enjoy it," "I always have something to watch." Neither is wrong - but only one is the BELT+POLE customer.

The Spectrum

Utility and entertainment are not binary. Most products sit somewhere on a spectrum, and some products serve both jobs for different segments.

What Drives Retention: The Key Difference

What churn looks likeCustomer leaves when P is no longer felt, or when E-Accrued becomes invisible.Customer leaves when content supply drops, a better feed emerges, or social graph migrates.

Why Entertainment Products Resist This Framework

BELT starts with B - behaviour despite friction. Entertainment customers do not have friction. They are not trying to solve a problem. They are choosing between enjoyable options. There is no workaround to find because there is no obstacle to overcome.

E starts with an enduring outcome. Entertainment does not produce a destination. A Netflix user is not accumulating progress toward a life change. They are consuming content. The hundredth show is not more valuable than the first.

The entertainment trap
Many product teams build utility products but measure them like entertainment products - maximising session length, daily active users, and content consumption. When utility products optimise for engagement rather than outcome progress, they accidentally build Metric Features. Calm's streak is the most cited example: it optimises sessions opened, not felt calm. The metric is entertainment-native. The product is utility.

The Hybrid Problem: Spotify

Spotify is the most instructive edge case. It sits in the middle of the spectrum because it can be experienced either way by the same person at different moments.

The Spotify design choice
Spotify chose to design for the utility segment - the music lover who cares about taste evolution - even though entertainment listeners are a larger share of users. This choice is what produced Discover Weekly. Designing for the utility segment created a moat. Designing for the entertainment segment would have produced a better playlist interface. Only one of those compounds.

Where This Framework Applies

Applies fully

Utility software with an enduring problem, an active switching decision, and repeated use.

Fintech, productivity, health tools, professional tools, booking platforms. Grammarly, Notion, Strava, Wise, TurboTax, Superhuman, Rippling.

Applies partially

Marketplace products (liquidity dynamics matter alongside value). Network-dominant products (moat is other people, not product capability). Hardware with ecosystem defaults.

Airbnb, Uber, LinkedIn, Spotify (entertainment users), Kindle, iPhone.

Does not apply

Entertainment products where dopamine loops, content supply, and algorithmic compulsion drive retention. Products adopted purely through ecosystem bundling or distribution default. Genuinely transient products.

Netflix, TikTok, Instagram (consumption mode), gaming with no persistent outcome.

Four Diagnostic Questions Before You Begin

Is This a Utility Product? Four Questions
1. Can a loyal customer name a specific outcome? Not "I enjoy it" - a specific destination. "I am becoming a more confident writer." "My team has no email backlog." If yes: utility framing is valid. If no: question whether BELT will find an enduring problem worth designing for.
2. Is there a workaround? Does the bright-spot customer do something manual, effortful, and specific to solve this problem without your product? If yes: utility signal. If no: the product may be satisfying a want, not solving a problem.
3. Does the hundredth use deliver more value than the first? T question. If yes: the product is Enabling and E architecture can compound. If no: the product is closer to entertainment - each session is equally disposable.
4. What would a customer lose by stopping? Utility: something irreplaceable they built - their data, history, progress. Entertainment: they would miss it, but nothing is lost. The loss test is the sharpest distinction between the two.
Introduction Summary: Where This Framework Applies
Utility products solve a specific recurring problem. Entertainment products deliver hedonic value. Only utility products have the properties BELT+POLE is designed to diagnose and design for.
The framework applies fully to utility products, partially to marketplaces and network-dominant products, and not at all to pure entertainment products.
The most dangerous position: building a utility product but measuring it with entertainment metrics. Session length and DAU are entertainment metrics. P-state confirmation and E-Accrued are utility metrics.
Spotify is the instructive hybrid: the utility segment (taste evolution) produces the moat (Discover Weekly). The entertainment segment (background listening) produces fragile retention. Spotify chose to design for the utility segment. That choice is Discover Weekly.
Four diagnostic questions before you begin: Can a loyal customer name a specific outcome? Is there a workaround? Does the hundredth use deliver more value? What would they lose by stopping?
Part One
BELT: Diagnosis Before Design
The most common product design mistake is solving the wrong problem well. BELT is the diagnostic engine that prevents this. It forces four specific questions before any design decision is made.
The BELT Sequence

BELT is a sequence. Each question builds on the previous. Answering out of order produces unreliable results.

Chapter 1
The Diagnosis That Changes Everything
Most product failures are not execution failures. They are diagnosis failures.

In 2013, Robinhood launched with a founding thesis that would shape every product decision for the next decade: young adults are not investing because commissions are too high. Remove the commission. The market opens up. Problem solved.

The thesis was plausible. It was logical. It was wrong.

Young adults were not staying out of financial markets because of $9.99 commissions. They were staying out because they did not know what to buy and were terrified of making the wrong decision. The barrier was financial confidence, not financial access. These are different problems. They require different products.

Robinhood built for the wrong problem with enormous competence. The result: confetti on trade execution, gamified leaderboards, 24-hour trading, push notifications for every price movement. Every feature was engineered well. Every feature was wrong. The product optimised for more trades when it should have been building financial confidence. No amount of execution excellence can fix a diagnosis failure.

"When the obstacle thesis is wrong, every feature built on it is wrong - regardless of execution quality. The wrong diagnosis is not a small mistake. It is the entire architecture."

This is not a story about a bad product team. Robinhood's engineering and design were exceptional. This is a story about what happens when you skip diagnosis and move directly to design. The team was answering the wrong question brilliantly.

The Pattern Is Not Rare

Robinhood is the most visible example of a failure pattern that appears in almost every product that plateaus. The team identifies a market. They begin designing. Momentum builds. Features ship. Metrics improve in the short term. Then retention flattens. The team diagnoses a retention problem and ships more features. Nothing changes.

The retention problem is real. But it is a symptom. The root cause is the diagnosis that happened before the first line of code was written - or did not happen at all.

The uncomfortable truth
Most product teams diagnose the problem they want to solve, not the problem the customer actually has. User interviews confirm assumptions more often than they challenge them. Feature requests describe symptoms, not causes. The only reliable starting point is behaviour - what the customer is doing despite friction, without your product.

BELT: Four Questions That Force the Right Diagnosis

BELT is not a framework for generating ideas. It is a framework for stress-testing the idea you already have. It forces four specific questions in sequence. Each one is designed to surface the assumption most likely to be wrong.

QuestionWhat it surfacesWhat it kills
B - What are they doing despite friction?The workaround. Proof that the problem is real and unsolved.Hypothetical customers. Problems that exist only in interviews.
E - What outcome do they actually need?The life change. The destination, not the feature.Feature-level P. Products that optimise the mechanism instead of the outcome.
L - Why have they not switched?The specific barrier. What liberation must remove.Generic onboarding. Liberation designed for the wrong lock-in type.
T - Transient or Enabling?Whether depth value can form. Whether E architecture is possible.E investment on Transient products. Moats that can never form.

Applied to Robinhood: B surfaces young adults paper-trading on Reddit spreadsheets - real demand confirmed. E asks what outcome they actually need, and the honest answer is financial confidence, not trade execution. L identifies the barrier as activation inertia ("what if I pick the wrong stock?") not commission cost. T asks whether the hundredth trade should be more informed than the first - yes, if P is confidence, making this Enabling. Robinhood built for the wrong E and the wrong T simultaneously.

The Sequence Is Not Optional

Each question depends on the previous one. B confirms there is real demand before you theorise about the problem. E names the destination before you design toward it. L identifies the specific barrier before you claim to remove it. T determines whether the entire E architecture is architecturally possible.

Skip B and you design for a hypothetical customer. Skip E and you design at feature level. Skip L and your onboarding removes the wrong barrier. Skip T and you spend 18 months building a moat that cannot form.

What BELT is not
BELT is not a customer discovery template. It is not a list of interview questions. It is a stress test applied to your current hypothesis. Run it on a product you are already building. The gaps it surfaces are almost always already visible in your retention data - they just have not been named yet.
Chapter Summary: The Diagnosis That Changes Everything
Robinhood is the defining case: wrong obstacle thesis, wrong E architecture, wrong features - regardless of execution quality. The diagnosis failure happened before the first feature was designed.
Most product failures are not execution failures. They are diagnosis failures. The team was answering the wrong question brilliantly.
BELT forces four questions in sequence: B (workaround), E (life change), L (specific barrier), T (Transient or Enabling). Each one is designed to surface the assumption most likely to be wrong.
The sequence is non-negotiable. Each question depends on the previous. Skipping any one produces a specific, predictable failure mode downstream.
Chapter 2
B - Behaviour
What are they doing despite friction?

The most reliable signal of real demand is not a survey. It is a person who has already built their own solution to the problem you are trying to solve.

The Bright Spot Method

Before any product exists, some customers solve the problem themselves. They log food in Notes apps. They record Loom-like videos in Zoom and share the link. They build finance spreadsheets that track their portfolio in ways apps do not. These are bright spots.

B asks
"What are they doing despite the friction of doing it manually?" The answer defines your most valuable early customer.
The Bright Spot Interview - Three Questions
"Walk me through the last time you dealt with [the problem]. What did you actually do?"
"What tools or workarounds are you currently using?" (Never ask if they have a workaround - ask what their workaround is.)
"What would you lose if you had to stop using that workaround tomorrow?" This is the most important question. The answer reveals the E architecture in embryonic form.

The FUD Score

Score the intensity of the problem using three dimensions. Each scores 1-3. Maximum score: 27. Products with FUD below 9 rarely sustain motivation long enough for E to compound.

Score = F x U x D. Max: 27. Above 18: strong structural demand. Below 9: reconsider the problem.

Exercise: Finding Your Bright Spots
Apply to your current product or a product you are building.
Describe the specific workaround your most valuable early customers were using before your product existed.
Score the workaround on FUD. Calculate the product.
What would those customers lose if they had to abandon your product tomorrow? Is that loss the moat you designed - or a happy accident?
Chapter Summary: B - Behaviour
Bright spots are customers who solved the problem with a workaround. Most reliable signal of real demand.
Three interview questions. The third ("what would you lose?") is the most important.
FUD score = Frequency x Urgency x Distinctness. Above 18: structural demand. Below 9: insufficient motivation.
Non-obvious truth
"User feedback over-indexes on transient pain. The problem that generates the loudest complaints is almost never the enduring problem worth solving. Listen to what customers do, not what they say."
Chapter 3
E - Enduring
What outcome do they actually need?

Most products are designed at feature level. The Enduring question forces the team to name the specific life change the customer is trying to produce. Everything downstream depends on getting this right.

Three Levels of Problem Definition

LevelDescriptionExample (fitness)
FeatureWhat the product does."Log your calories."
OutcomeWhat the customer achieves."Lose 10kg in 12 weeks."
Life ChangeHow the customer's life is different."Understand your body so tracking becomes unnecessary."
P Statement Template
"For [specific segment], [product] delivers [specific outcome], so [life is different in this specific way]." Test: could this appear in a feature list? If yes, rewrite at Life Change level.

P Statement Examples

ProductWrong P (Feature level)Right P (Life Change level)
MyFitnessPal"Track your calories.""For adults who have tried and failed through willpower alone, MFP delivers insight into how food choices affect how they feel - so tracking becomes unnecessary."
Strava"Track your runs and rides.""For endurance athletes who train consistently, Strava delivers visible proof that fitness is compounding - so effort has meaning beyond completion."
Robinhood"Commission-free trading.""For young adults building financial confidence, Robinhood delivers a first real investment from knowledge, not impulse."
Exercise: Writing Your P Statement
Most product teams have never written P at Life Change level.
Write your current P statement exactly as it appears in your documentation.
Apply the three tests: Feature list? Specific segment? Life change?
Rewrite using the template: "For [segment], [product] delivers [outcome], so [life changes in this specific way]."
Show both versions to a customer. Which would they tell a friend?
Chapter Summary: E - Enduring
Three levels: Feature, Outcome, Life Change. P must be at Life Change level.
If P could appear in a feature list, it is at the wrong altitude.
Enduring vs Transient: not all problems sustain motivation. E architecture only works for enduring problems.
Chapter 4
L - Lock-in
Why have they not switched?

Every customer who has not yet adopted your product is paying some cost to stay where they are. L names that cost and removes it.

Three Lock-in Types

Lock-in typeWhat L must doWhat L must not do
Switching CostMake migration effortless. Import history. Prove nothing is lost."Easy to get started." That is not the barrier.
Search CostShow the outcome before commitment. Remove evaluation risk.Migration tools. There is nothing to migrate.
InertiaReduce the first step to near-zero. No commitment before value.Onboarding requiring belief, goals, or commitment.
Chapter Summary: L - Lock-in
Three types: Switching Cost, Search Cost, Inertia. Each requires a different liberation design.
The wrong liberation design leaves the barrier intact. Generic ease of use is not L.
Most common mistake: onboarding reduces general friction instead of removing the specific barrier.
Non-obvious truth
"Lock-in matters more than delight in low-frequency products. A customer who loves your product but has no lock-in will leave the moment a competitor offers a better first session. A customer who is locked in will stay even when they are frustrated."
Chapter 5
T - Type
Transient or Enabling?

T is the most consequential question in the diagnostic. The answer determines whether an E architecture is possible at all.

TransientEnabling
The hundredth use delivers the same value as the first.The hundredth use is more valuable than the first.
No data accumulates. No moat forms.Personal data, history, or patterns accumulate with each use.
Retention relies on P-Salience, not depth value.Retention compounds. Leaving means losing something irreplaceable.
Wise for a tourist: one transfer, no history.Wise for an expat: 24 months of multi-currency infrastructure.
The T test
"If a customer stopped using the product for six months and came back, would the product know them? Would the 601st session feel different from the first?" If yes: Enabling. If no: Transient.
Chapter Summary: T - Type
Enabling: the hundredth use is more valuable than the first. Transient: every session delivers the same value.
Building E architecture on a Transient product is wasted investment.
Cyclical and episodic Enabling products require deliberate Stage 8 (Reinforcement) design - Wrapped, Year in Sport.
Non-obvious truth
"If your product is solving a transient problem, you do not have a retention problem. You have a diagnosis problem. No feature, no onboarding improvement, and no re-engagement campaign will fix it. The only fix is a different problem."
Chapter 6
Segmentation
The BELT definition

BELT produces a different kind of segmentation - diagnostic rather than demographic, and therefore directly actionable.

Chapter 7
P - The Destination
The 12-month outcome

P is not a tagline. It is the specific outcome the customer experiences after sustained use. It is the destination that every other POLE element is moving the customer toward.

P and Outcome Types

TypeDefinitionExample
OngoingP delivered in every session. Customer renews because each session is worth having.Spotify: music finds you before you know you want it. Every session is P-state confirmation.
TerminalP delivered once. Customer achieves a specific outcome and the relationship transitions.Hinge: deleted the app. TurboTax: refund arrived. Atlys: visa in hand before deadline.
IntermittentP recurs at natural intervals. Customer returns when the condition returns.Hipcamp: every campsite matches what was described - each trip.
"Kingfisher had a tagline. IndiGo had a system."
The difference between perception as a strategy and delivery as a strategy.

The HOW Challenge

The HOW Audit
Step 1: Write the assumed HOW. "Every product in this market assumed value = [mechanism]."
Step 2: Challenge it. "What if value is actually [different mechanism]?"
Step 3: Ask what if that's wrong. Surface the assumed mechanism and test whether your product also assumes it.
Spotify: Every platform assumed "music = something you find." Spotify: "what if music finds you?" This HOW challenge reframes P, O, and E simultaneously.
Better.com: Every lender assumed "value = lowest rate." Better: "what if value is certainty and speed, not the rate?" Same category, different HOW.
Chapter Summary: P - The Destination
P is the specific life change after enough use. Not a tagline. Not a feature description.
Three types: Ongoing, Terminal, Intermittent.
P at Feature level cascades: wrong O, wrong L, wrong E.
HOW audit: challenge the assumed mechanism in your market before writing P.
Chapter 8
O - The Belief Moment
Understandable before first use. Verifiable after first use.

O is the moment a new customer first believes that P is possible. It must fire in session 1. If it does not, the customer leaves before E can begin.

O and the Obstacle Thesis

The obstacle thesis names the specific reason the customer has not already solved the problem. O must break exactly the belief the obstacle thesis identifies.

Obstacle Thesis to O
The obstacle thesis names the belief. O breaks it. If you cannot trace O back to the obstacle thesis, O is not designed - it is a default.

Three O Failure Modes

O Failure Modes
O fires too late: belief-breaking moment exists but takes too long. Day 7 retention low even when Month 3 is strong. Fix: move O to session 1.
O fires for the wrong segment: designed for early adopters, not for the segment now being acquired. Fix: redesign O for the current acquisition segment.
O never fires: no belief-breaking moment exists. Users explore and leave. Fix: name the specific belief on arrival. Design one moment that breaks it.
Chapter Summary: O - The Belief Moment
O breaks the sceptical belief on arrival. Must fire in session 1.
Understandable before first use. Verifiable after first use.
Derived from the obstacle thesis. Cannot be traced to obstacle = not designed.
Three failure modes: too late, wrong segment, never fires.
Chapter 9
L - Liberation
Removes the old barrier. Enables adoption without rebuild.

L is not easy onboarding. It is the deliberate removal of the specific barrier keeping this segment out. Generic ease of use is not L.

Lock-in typeWhat L must doExample
Switching CostImport. Data portability. "Your history comes with you."Spotify imports Apple Music playlists. Taste model begins learning from existing history.
Search CostShow the outcome before commitment. Reverse trial.Warby Parker home try-on. TurboTax shows refund before filing.
InertiaEliminate the first-step requirement. Zero-commitment start."Log one meal. No calorie target required." MFP's current onboarding asks for a goal in session 1 - exactly the wrong design.
Chapter Summary: L - Liberation
L traces directly to the lock-in type from BELT diagnosis.
Three types produce three different liberation designs.
Type 1 Convergence: O and L fire in the same moment. Type 2: same session, sequential.
Chapter 10
E - The Retention Arc
The 5-step E Design Ladder

E is not "the retention features." E is the architecture that makes leaving increasingly costly - by ensuring each session deposits value that did not exist before.

E-Delivered and E-Accrued

E-Delivered (per session)E-Accrued (across sessions)
What it isFunctional VP + Experience VP in each session.The data asset the customer has built. The moat.
What it drivesDay 1 to Day 30 retention.Month 3+ retention and renewal.
FailureSessions feel adequate but customer cannot describe what they achieved.Moat exists but is invisible. Switching cost felt only when leaving.
Critical dependency
Most teams invest in E-Accrued before E-Delivered is working. The moat forms over a gap that churns customers before they reach it.

The 5-Step E Design Ladder

Metric Features vs Micro-P

Micro-P (passes Step 5)Metric Feature (fails Step 5)
"You caught 34 errors this week, down from 51." - Grammarly"Your profile is 80% complete."
"Your pace improved 12% over 8 weeks." - Strava"You completed your 10th ride!"
"Discover Weekly accuracy up - 7 tracks saved vs 2 last week." - Spotify"You have listened 200 hours this year."

Three Advanced Diagnostics

HOW Audit (pre-POLE)
Surface the assumed mechanism. Ask: how is value created in the current system? Then: what if that's wrong?
Example: Robinhood assumed the mechanism was access (zero commissions). HOW Audit: the real mechanism should be confidence formation. Every E feature built on the access mechanism compounds the wrong HOW.
POLE Tension Test (within POLE)
For each POLE element: can you write two genuinely different plausible answers?
If no: the BELT framing is too shallow. Return to diagnosis before proceeding.
"Shallow problems have obvious solutions. Deep problems have competing correct solutions."
System Effects Check (post-POLE)
"If we are right about POLE, what new problems does this create?"
Stopping rule: good design only if Tension, Leverage, and Testability all pass.
Example: Spotify's E design creates a new problem - podcast users get a degraded music taste model. The podcast section undermines the HOW. System Effects Check surfaces this.
Exercise: E Design - Run the Ladder
Apply to your current product. This exercise often reveals that your strongest-performing features are Metric Features.
Decompose your P into 3-5 components. What must be true in each session?
Set the floor for each component.
Name the load-bearing Experience VP dimension. Which one addresses the obstacle thesis?
Apply Step 5 to your three highest-engagement features. Can each be traced to Step 1 in two steps?
Chapter Summary: E - The Retention Arc
E-Delivered must work before E-Accrued can form. Invest in the right order.
5-step ladder: decompose P, set floor, derive Experience VP, Referral VP moment, cut Metric Features.
Micro-P carries customers across the Valley of Death. Task completion does not.
Three advanced diagnostics: HOW Audit, POLE Tension Test, System Effects Check.
Referral VP fires when P is realised. Three types: Financial, Social, Personal. Three actors: Generator, Distributor, Receiver.
Non-obvious truth
"Most PMs are optimising the acquisition layer while the retention layer collapses underneath them. Strong O + weak E is a leaky bucket. You can keep filling it. It will keep leaking. Fix E first."
Part Three
Value Currency
Chapter 11
Value Currency: Time, Data, Money
What accumulates. What forms the moat.

Every product accumulates at least one currency. The moat only forms when the customer can feel what they have built. If the moat is invisible, it does not protect.

Time
The seed.
Sessions invested. Weakest alone - a competitor can buy it back with a discount. Only becomes load-bearing when it enables Data to form.
Spotify streams. Strava logs. Duolingo days practiced.
Data
The moat.
Irreplaceable personalisation built from the customer's own behaviour. Cannot be transferred. The competitor cannot replicate it without the customer's entire history.
Spotify taste model. Notion knowledge graph. Superhuman triage patterns.
Money
The lock.
Activates last. The customer pays to protect what has been built - the data asset they cannot rebuild elsewhere. Not a paywall; protection for the moat they have created.
Spotify Premium. Notion team plan. LinkedIn Recruiter.

The Sequence Is Not Universal

Most products progress Time to Data to Money. But the starting currency depends on what the customer deposits first.

Chapter Summary: Value Currency
Three currencies: Time (seed), Data (moat), Money (lock). Direction always toward the moat.
Sequence is product-specific. LinkedIn starts with Data. Robinhood starts with Money.
The moat only protects when the customer can feel it. Invisible moats do not retain.
Three failure modes: Stuck at Time, Data Invisible, Premature Money.

The Value Lifecycle
Nine stages. Three actors. Design for all of them.

Value does not stop at delivery. It must be perceived, received, realised, reinforced, and captured to complete the cycle. Most products design for Stages 1-4 and leave everything else to chance.

Nine stages. Three actors. Each stage has a primary owner.
Stage 8 (Reinforcement) is the highest-leverage gap in most roadmaps.

Chapter 13
The Weakest Link Audit
One weak element caps everything else

POLE elements compound each other. A strong E cannot compensate for a weak P. The weakest link determines the ceiling of the entire product.

The Audit

Score each POLE element on two dimensions. Clarity: can a customer state this element clearly without prompting? Credibility: do customers believe it based on actual experience?

ScoreWhat it means
High (H)Most customers can state it clearly and believe it from experience.
Medium (M)Some customers can state it. Credibility is partial or inconsistent.
Low (L)Most customers cannot articulate it, or do not believe it from experience.
The rule
The element with the lowest combined score is the Weakest Link. It is your only priority. Never fix H elements when an L exists.

Fix Sequence: P First. Always.

Fix Order
P is L: fix P before anything else. O cannot fire for a clear belief when P is undefined. L cannot remove the right barrier when P is wrong. E has nothing to compound.
O is L: fix after P is confirmed.
L is L: fix after O is confirmed.
E is L: fix last. E failure is almost always a symptom of weak P, not a root cause.

LinkedIn: A Worked Example

900 million users. The Weakest Link is not where most product teams would look.

P

Application Guide
Run the Diagnostic in 30 Minutes
Five steps. One output. Applied to any product you know.

Every concept in this book points here. The framework is only valuable when it is in contact with a real product. This guide takes you from blank page to diagnosis in 30 minutes.

You will need: one product you know well, 30 minutes, a place to write. Nothing else.
1
5 min
B - Find the behaviour

Write down: what is the specific workaround your most committed early customers used before your product existed? Make it concrete. "They used spreadsheets" is not specific enough. "They maintained a colour-coded spreadsheet with 47 rows of manually tracked data that they updated every Sunday night" is specific enough.

If you cannot name a specific workaround: stop here. You do not have confirmed demand. You have a hypothesis. Validate B before running the rest of this guide.
2
5 min
E - Name the life change

Complete this sentence: "For [specific segment], [product] delivers [specific outcome], so [life is different in this specific way]." Read it back. Could it appear in a feature list? If yes, you are at feature level. Rewrite it at life change level.

Then answer: is this problem enduring or transient? If the problem resolves itself after first use, or if the customer has no reason to return once P is delivered, it is transient. Name this honestly.

Transient is not a failure. It is information. A transient problem requires a different product strategy. Do not build E architecture on a transient product.
3
5 min
L - Identify the lock-in type

Which one describes your segment? Switching Cost (prior investment in a competitor that cannot be transferred), Search Cost (evaluation anxiety - they do not know if the alternative is better), or Inertia (they have never started - the barrier is belief, not migration).

Now look at your current onboarding. Write one sentence describing what it does to address this specific barrier. If the sentence describes generic setup or feature orientation rather than the specific lock-in type you just named: your L is misdiagnosed.

4
5 min
T - Validate the type

Answer one question: if a customer stopped using your product for six months and came back, would the product know them? Would the 601st session feel different from the first?

If yes: Enabling. Build the E architecture. The moat can form. If no: Transient. Do not invest in personalisation or depth value features. They will not compound. Design for P-Salience instead: make your product the obvious choice at the moment the problem recurs.

5
10 min
Score your POLE

Score each element High, Medium, or Low on two questions: Clarity (can a customer state this element clearly?) and Credibility (do customers believe it from experience?).

ElementClarity scoreCredibility scoreCombined
PH / M / LH / M / L
OH / M / LH / M / L
LH / M / LH / M / L
EH / M / LH / M / L

The element with the lowest combined score is your Weakest Link. That is your only priority. Fix it before investing in any other element.

Your output at the end of 30 minutes
You will have named:
The specific workaround (B)
The life change at outcome level (E)
The problem type: transient or enduring (T)
The lock-in type (L)
The weakest POLE element
You will know:
Whether E architecture is possible
Whether your onboarding removes the right barrier
Which single change would have the highest impact
Whether your product is worth building the moat for

Run this guide on the same product twice: once with what you believe to be true, and once based purely on what your data shows. The gap between those two runs is your product's most important strategic problem.
