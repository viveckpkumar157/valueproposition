Before You Begin

The Four Jobs

Why every product needs four architectures, not one

Every product must do four jobs. Not four features. Four separate jobs, each done at a different moment, for a different reason, evaluated by a completely different standard.

Most products attempt all four jobs with one statement. They write a value proposition, optimise it, test it against competitors, and ship it everywhere. The acquisition deck, the landing page, the onboarding screen, the renewal email: one message, four jobs. This is the structural fault this book is built against.

1
Give a new customer a reason to believe
"Why should I believe this product can change anything for me?"

Must fire in session 1. Must break a specific sceptical belief. Cannot be earned over time. If this job is not done in the first 30 seconds, the customer leaves before anything else can happen.

POLE: O: The Belief Moment
2
Remove the specific barrier to switching
"I am not using this because..."

Every non-adopter has a reason. It is specific. It is structural. Generic ease of use does not address it. This job requires knowing exactly which type of lock-in is keeping them out, then removing it precisely.

POLE: L: Liberation
3
Deliver an outcome worth staying for
"What is permanently different in my life if this works?"

Not a feature. Not a metric. A specific life change that a named segment cares about enough to return for. This is the destination the entire product is moving the customer toward. Everything else is in service of it.

POLE: P: The Destination
4
Become more valuable the longer they use it
"What would I lose if I stopped?"

This is the only job that compounds. Each session deposits value that did not exist before, until leaving means losing something irreplaceable. This is the moat. It is built through use, not promised at acquisition.

POLE: E: The Retention Arc

BELT is the diagnostic engine that tells you which version of each job your product must do, and for whom. POLE is the architecture for doing all four jobs deliberately, instead of doing one and hoping the rest follow.

The constraint

The four jobs are not independent. They compete. You cannot optimise all four at the same time with the same resources. Every decision that strengthens one creates pressure somewhere else.

When a job is not designed, it is compensated for.

Weak belief becomes an education problem.
Weak switching becomes a feature problem.
Weak outcome becomes a usage problem.
Weak compounding becomes a re-engagement problem.

The symptoms look different. The cause is the same. A job was not designed. It was compensated for.

The failure pattern

Most products fail with one of these structures:

PatternWhat it looks like
Strong belief, weak outcomeHigh acquisition, low retention. Users try and leave.
Strong outcome, weak switchingAdmiration without adoption. People love the idea but don't switch.
Strong usage, weak compoundingActivity without value. People return but nothing builds.
Strong compounding, wrong outcomeA moat around nothing. Deep engagement with the wrong destination.

The product looks healthy. Until it doesn't. The team responds by improving execution. The product improves. The system does not.

"Most products do not fail because they lack value. They fail because they assign the wrong job to the wrong moment, and then try to fix it with effort."

Read the BELT section before writing a single POLE element. The quality of each job is entirely determined by the quality of your diagnosis. Skip the diagnosis and you are designing four architectures for the wrong customer.

Foreword

The Wrong Choice, Made Confidently

Why most product failure is a value selection problem, not an execution problem

In 2013, Robinhood made a decision.

Young adults are not investing because commissions are too high. Remove the commission. The market opens up.

The logic was clean. The conviction was real. The execution was strong.

It was also wrong.

Users signed up. They explored. They left. Not because the product was badly built. Not because the team moved slowly. Because the problem was misdiagnosed.

The barrier was not cost. It was confidence. People were not avoiding markets because they could not afford to enter. They were avoiding them because they did not know what to do once inside. Robinhood removed the door fee. It did not remove the fear of the room.

"Most products do not fail because teams build badly. They fail because teams choose the wrong value to build, and then execute that choice with complete confidence."

This is the failure mode nobody talks about clearly, because it is invisible until it is too late. A team that builds the wrong feature knows it quickly. A team that builds the wrong type of value proposition can spend years, and tens of millions, before the diagnosis becomes undeniable.

Not poor execution. Not lack of effort. Not bad teams. A wrong decision, made early, made cleanly, made with confidence. Everything that followed was correct. And everything that followed made the product worse.

Execution does not fix a bad decision. It multiplies it. The better the team, the faster they build. The faster they build, the deeper they commit. The deeper they commit, the harder it becomes to see the mistake. By the time the signal appears: low retention, confused usage, stalled growth. The product is already shaped around the wrong idea.

The root cause is almost always the same: single value proposition thinking. The assumption that one well-crafted statement can simultaneously explain why a customer should try your product, why they should stay, why switching is too costly, and what their life looks like after sustained use. It cannot. Each job requires its own architecture. Conflating them is how Robinhood happened.

Every product has four value propositions. Each does a specific job. Each must be designed separately. Each can fail independently. If you design one and assume the others will follow, you will build a product that works, just enough, until it doesn't.

"Your product does not have one value proposition. It has four. Each does a different job at a different moment. Designing one and ignoring the others is not a shortcut. It is a structural fault."

This book is a system for making that architecture explicit before you build. It has two parts. BELT is the diagnostic engine: four questions that force you to classify the type of problem you are solving before any design decision is made. POLE is the design architecture: four value propositions, each doing a specific job at a specific moment in the customer's journey.

Kingfisher had a tagline. IndiGo had a system. One competed on perception. The other competed on delivery. Only one is still flying. The difference is not budget or talent or market conditions. The difference is whether the team built a slogan or built an architecture. This book is about the architecture.

Do not skip BELT to get to POLE. The sequence is the point. Do not skip the Introduction: the utility vs entertainment distinction is the first gate, not optional context. Introduction

Utility products, entertainment products, and the difference that determines everything

Before you use this framework, answer one question.

What kind of product is this?

If you get this wrong, everything that follows will look correct, and fail in practice. This is not a warm-up question. It is a gate. The answer determines whether the framework applies, how it applies, and where its limits are.

The Core Distinction

This distinction was developed by Ravi Mehta (ex-CPO, Tinder). The framing here builds on his original taxonomy of utility vs entertainment products.

A utility product solves a specific, recurring problem. The customer uses it because it moves them toward a defined outcome they would pursue even without the product. The product is instrumental. Value is measured in progress.

An entertainment product delivers hedonic value. The customer uses it because the experience itself is enjoyable. There is no destination. There is no problem being solved. Value is measured in engagement, not progress.

The Test

Ask a loyal customer: "What specific outcome does this product help you achieve?" A utility customer can answer this. An entertainment customer gives you consumption language: "It's entertaining," "I enjoy it," "I always have something to watch." Neither is wrong, but only one is the BELT+POLE customer.

The Entertainment Trap

Many product teams build utility products but measure them like entertainment products, maximising session length, daily active users, and content consumption. When utility products optimise for engagement rather than outcome progress, they design features that move a metric without advancing P-state. Calm's streak is the most cited example: it optimises sessions opened, not felt calm. The metric is entertainment-native. The product is utility.

The opposite failure exists too. Some teams build entertainment products and force utility logic onto them. They define outcomes that do not exist. They build tracking where no progress is required. They search for compounding where none can form. The product becomes heavier without becoming better.

If the customer is not trying to get somewhere, you are not building a utility product. And this framework will not save you.

The strategic implication of getting this wrong: If your product does not create progress, you cannot build compounding value. If you cannot build compounding value, you cannot create a moat through use. If you cannot create a moat, you are competing on attention. And attention is rented.

Four Diagnostic Questions Before You Begin

  1. Can a loyal customer name a specific outcome? Not "I enjoy it": a specific destination. If yes: utility framing is valid.
  2. Is there a workaround? Does the bright-spot customer do something manual, effortful, and specific to solve this problem without your product? If yes: utility signal.
  3. Does the hundredth use deliver more value than the first? If yes: Enabling. If no: closer to entertainment.
  4. What would a customer lose by stopping? Utility: something irreplaceable they built. Entertainment: they would miss it, but nothing is lost.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 1

The Diagnosis That Changes Everything

Diagnosis determines all four jobs.

Most product failures are not execution failures. They are diagnosis failures.

In 2013, Robinhood made a decision. Young adults are not investing because commissions are too high. Remove the commission. The market opens up.

The logic was sound. The data supported it. The team executed well. The decision was wrong.

Users signed up. They explored. They left. Not because the product was badly built. Not because the team moved slowly. Because the barrier was misidentified. The problem was not cost. It was confidence. People were not staying out of markets because they could not afford to enter. They were staying out because they did not know what to do once inside.

Robinhood removed friction. It did not remove uncertainty.

"When the obstacle thesis is wrong, every feature built on it is wrong, regardless of execution quality. The wrong diagnosis is not a small mistake. It is the entire architecture."

This is where the failure happens. Before the roadmap. Before the features. Before the first line of code. At the moment the team decides what problem they are solving. If that decision is wrong, everything that follows is correct, and useless.

"Execution does not fix a bad diagnosis. It scales it."

The better the team, the faster they build. The faster they build, the deeper they commit. The deeper they commit, the harder it becomes to question the premise. By the time the signal appears: low retention, confused usage, stalled growth. The product is already shaped around the wrong idea. Most teams never see this clearly. Because they are not looking for it. They are trained to debug execution: improve onboarding, refine messaging, add features, increase acquisition. All reasonable. All downstream. The problem was upstream.

The pattern

Most failed products share the same structure:

A plausible idea. Strong execution. Early traction. Gradual decay.

The team responds by improving execution. The decay continues. Because the system is solving the wrong problem well.

Product quality is not defined by how well you build. It is defined by how accurately you choose. BELT is the system for making that choice correctly. POLE is how you execute it once the choice is right. Get BELT wrong, and POLE becomes precision applied to the wrong problem. Get BELT right, and POLE becomes obvious.

Chapter Summary

Robinhood: wrong obstacle thesis. Wrong E architecture. Wrong features. Regardless of execution quality.

Most product failures are not execution failures. They are diagnosis failures. The team was answering the wrong question brilliantly. The better the team, the faster the wrong direction.

BELT is not a framework for building. It is a system for preventing the wrong decision. It tells you what not to build. Four questions in sequence, each one eliminates a class of mistake.

What follows BELT: POLE, which engineers four separate value propositions, one for each job the product must do. Get the diagnosis right, and the design becomes obvious.

Your best growth quarter may be the quarter you validated the wrong thesis at scale.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 2

B: Behaviour

What are they doing despite friction?

Confirms a job worth solving exists.

Before you define the problem, answer this: what is the customer already doing to solve it?

Not what they say. Not what they intend. What they actually do.

If nothing exists, no workaround, no behaviour, no repeated attempt, there is no problem worth building for. There is only interest. And interest does not convert.

"Interest is what customers say. Behaviour is what they pay for."
"Customers do not invent workarounds for problems they do not care about."

The Bright Spot Method

Every meaningful product begins the same way. A small group of customers is already doing the thing, despite the friction. They are slower than they want, using the wrong tools, stitching together systems, repeating effort. But they are doing it. These are your first customers. Not because they said they would use your product. Because they already built one.

B asks: "What are they doing despite the friction of doing it manually?" The answer defines your most valuable early customer. Without B, you build for hypothetical users, stated preferences, imagined demand. It feels convincing. Until it meets reality.

Two Bright Spots in Practice

ProductThe workaround (the bright spot)What it confirms
Calendly Professionals sending chains of 5–8 emails to schedule a single meeting. "Are you free Tuesday?" "Tuesday is bad, how about Thursday?" "Thursday works: morning or afternoon?" The workaround is elaborate, universal, and embarrassing enough that people joke about it. The scheduling pain is real and the market is enormous. Nobody does this because they enjoy it. They do it because there is no obvious, frictionless alternative they trust.
Notion Teams managing knowledge across a patchwork of Google Docs, Slack threads, email chains, and personal spreadsheets, each person maintaining their own system, none of it connected. When someone new joins, they spend two weeks asking "where is the process doc for X?" and getting a different answer each time. The knowledge management pain is real and structural. Teams are not doing this by choice. They are doing it because nothing has made the alternative feel worth the switch.

The Bright Spot Interview — Three Questions

  1. "Walk me through the last time you dealt with [the problem]. What did you actually do?"
  2. "What tools or workarounds are you currently using?" (Never ask if they have a workaround: ask what their workaround is.)
  3. "What would you lose if you had to stop using that workaround tomorrow?" This is the most important question. The answer reveals the E architecture in embryonic form.

The FUDU Score

Score the intensity of the problem using four dimensions. Each scores 1–3. The FUDU score tells you whether the problem is worth building for.

Before scoring, answer one gate question: Is this problem uniquely underserved? If the market already has good solutions, a high FUDU score builds a product that competes on execution, not architecture. Name the gap first. Then score the intensity.

F Frequency
How often does this pain recur?
Does the problem come back regardless of prior attempts to solve it?
1 = monthly  |  2 = weekly  |  3 = daily
U Ubiquity
How many people face this problem?
Is this niche, broad professional, or universal?
1 = niche  |  2 = broad professional  |  3 = universal
D Depth

FUDU summary for Calendly: Frequency: HIGH (multiple times per day). Ubiquity: HIGH (every professional who schedules meetings). Depth: HIGH (15–30 minutes wasted, credibility cost). Urgency: HIGH (the thread is live, the meeting is this week, cannot be deferred).

All four dimensions high. Worth building for.

"A problem that is frequent but shallow creates noise. A problem that is deep but rare creates spikes. Only problems that are frequent and deep create products."

Products with combined FUDU below 9 rarely sustain motivation long enough for E to compound. Above 18 across all four dimensions: structural demand. The problem is real, broad, painful, and urgent.

Exercise: Finding Your Bright Spots

  1. Describe the specific workaround your most valuable early customers were using before your product existed. Make it concrete: "they used spreadsheets" is not specific enough.
  2. Gate question: is this problem uniquely underserved? Who else is trying to solve it and how well?
  3. Score the problem on FUDU (1–3 each). Which dimensions are highest? Which is the weakest signal?
  4. What would those customers lose if they had to abandon your product tomorrow? Is that loss the moat you designed, or a happy accident?

Chapter Summary

Bright spots are customers who solved the problem with a workaround. Most reliable signal of real demand.

Three interview questions. The third ("what would you lose?") is the most important.

FUDU gate: before scoring, confirm the problem is uniquely underserved. Then score Frequency x Ubiquity x Depth x Urgency.

Non-obvious truth: user feedback over-indexes on transient pain. The problem that generates the loudest complaints is almost never the enduring problem worth solving. Listen to what customers do, not what they say.

The constraint: if the customer is not already doing something painful, they will not adopt something better. If there is no workaround, there is no product.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 3

E: Enduring

What outcome do they actually need?

Designs Job 3: the destination.

Most products are designed at feature level. The Enduring question forces the team to name the specific life change the customer is trying to produce. Everything downstream depends on getting this right.

Three Levels of Problem Definition

LevelDescriptionExample (fitness)
FeatureWhat the product does."Log your calories."
OutcomeWhat the customer achieves."Lose 10kg in 12 weeks."
Life ChangeHow the customer's life is different."Understand your body so tracking becomes unnecessary."

The Product Removal Test

Take your current value proposition. Remove the product from the description. What remains?

If nothing meaningful remains, you are describing a mechanism. Not a destination. If the value disappears with the product, you have not found the outcome. If the value remains after the product is removed, you are close to P.

The So What? Technique

The So What? technique forces the same escalation systematically. Most teams describe features when asked about P. Keep asking "so what?" until the answer stops mentioning the product. That is where the destination lives.

Loom: from feature claim to PLevel
"One video is worth a thousand words."Feature claim
So what?: "You can explain complex things without scheduling a meeting."Still about the product
So what?: "You can communicate decisions and context to anyone, anytime, asynchronously."Getting closer
So what?: "Your team operates at full speed regardless of time zone or availability."P: answer stops mentioning Loom

The rule: when the So What? answer stops mentioning the product, you have found P.

"If the outcome disappears when the product is removed, the value was never real."
"If your value proposition still needs the product to make sense, you have not found the value."

P Statement Template

"For [specific segment], [product] delivers [specific outcome], so [life is different in this specific way]."

Test: could this appear in a feature list? If yes, rewrite at Life Change level.

ProductWrong P (Feature level)Right P (Life Change level)
MyFitnessPal"Track your calories.""For adults who have tried and failed through willpower alone, MFP delivers a complete understanding of their body, so they know what to eat without having to think about it."
Strava"Track your runs and rides.""For endurance athletes who train consistently, Strava delivers visible proof that fitness is compounding, so effort has meaning beyond completion."
Robinhood"Commission-free trading.""For young adults building financial confidence, Robinhood delivers a first real investment from knowledge, not impulse."
Transient triggers acquisition. Enduring sustains value. A product needs both. The mistake is building E architecture (compounding data, habit loops, outcome tracking) for a problem that is only transient. The features exist. The investment was real. The moat never forms. This is not a retention problem. It is a diagnosis problem. The fix is not a better retention feature. The fix is a different problem classification.

Exercise: Writing Your P Statement

  1. Write your current P statement exactly as it appears in your documentation.
  2. Apply the three tests: Feature list? Specific segment? Life change?
  3. Run the So What? technique three times. Does the answer stop mentioning the product?
  4. Rewrite using the template: "For [segment], [product] delivers [outcome], so [life changes in this specific way]."
  5. Show both versions to a customer. Which would they tell a friend?

Chapter Summary

Three levels: Feature, Outcome, Life Change. P must be at Life Change level.

So What? technique: keep asking until the answer stops mentioning the product. That is P.

If P could appear in a feature list, it is at the wrong altitude.

Transient vs Enduring: not all problems sustain motivation. E architecture only works for enduring problems. If your product is solving a transient problem, no retention investment will fix it. Only a different problem classification will.

The line: customers do not stay for what the product does. They stay for what their life becomes. If you cannot describe what their life becomes, you have not found P.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 4

L: Lock-in Discovery

Why have they not switched? What is the cost keeping them where they are?

Identifies the barrier blocking Job 2.

If your product is genuinely better, why isn't the customer already using it?

Most teams answer incorrectly. They say: they don't know about us, our onboarding is not smooth enough, we need better messaging. These are not causes. They are symptoms.

Customers do not stay with worse products by accident. They stay because leaving costs something. Until that cost is removed, no improvement matters. A better product does not win. A product wins when the cost of switching is lower than the cost of staying.

"Customers do not switch when a better product exists. They switch when the cost of staying becomes visible."
"Customers do not compare products. They compare the cost of moving."

Lock-in is not loyalty. It is not satisfaction. It is not preference. It is accumulated cost. That cost takes different forms. If you do not identify which form exists, you will remove the wrong barrier. And nothing will change.

Three Lock-in Types

SC
Switching Cost
Prior investment in a competitor. History, data, integrations. Fix: migration. Import. Prove nothing is lost.
SC
Search Cost
Trapped by uncertainty. Evaluation anxiety, not migration. Fix: preview. Free trial. Show outcome before commitment.
I
Inertia
Never started. Belief barrier. "I am not the kind of person..." Fix: zero-commitment first step. No goal required to start.

One Product Per Type

TypeProductWhat the customer has already investedWhat liberation must do
Switching Cost Figma A design team has spent months building component libraries and design systems. Every new component deepens the investment. The cost of leaving is rebuilding months of work from scratch. Figma imports files directly. Nothing is lost. The team starts alongside existing tools.

The wrong liberation is worse than no liberation. Lock-in type determines liberation design. Get the type wrong and the barrier stays intact.

Teams design onboarding when they should be removing lock-in. Onboarding explains the product. Lock-in prevents switching. These are different problems with different solutions. If adoption is low and feedback is positive, the barrier was never removed. The product improved. The customer did not switch.

Exercise: Diagnosing Your Lock-in Type

  1. Which lock-in type describes your segment: Switching Cost, Search Cost, or Inertia?
  2. What does your current onboarding do to address this specific barrier?
  3. Identify your O+L relationship type: Convergence, Near-convergence, Sequential, or Independent.
  4. Ask five non-adopters why they have not switched. Does their answer confirm your diagnosis?
Non-obvious truth: Lock-in matters more than delight in low-frequency products. A customer who loves your product but has no lock-in will leave the moment a competitor offers a better first session. A customer who is locked in will stay even when they are frustrated.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 5

T: Type

Transient or Enabling? Do they come back, and does it matter more each time?

Determines whether Job 4 can exist.

Not every real problem is worth building a product for.

Some problems occur, get solved, and disappear. You can build for them. You cannot build on them. T is the most consequential question in the diagnostic because it determines the ceiling, not just whether E architecture is possible, but how far this product can go before you build it.

"You cannot compound value in a problem that does not repeat."

The question is not just transient or enabling. It is: does solving this problem make the next use more valuable, or does it reset to zero? If it resets, you are building a transaction. If it compounds, you are building a system.

Segmentation

The BELT definition

Defines which customer the four jobs apply to.

Most teams think they know their customer. They can describe age, industry, job title. They can name personas, cohorts, demographics.

None of this is segmentation.

Segmentation is not who the customer is. It is who experiences the problem in a way that cannot be ignored. If you build for the group, you build for the average. And the average does not adopt.

BELT defines the segment through the problem, not the person. A segment is a group with the same behaviour, the same desired outcome, the same barrier, and the same type of problem. If any of these differ, you are not looking at one segment. The pressure test: a real segment should make you uncomfortable, because it is narrow, because it excludes people, because it forces tradeoffs. If your segment feels safe, it is too broad.

At this point, you have answers.

Part Two — POLE  |  Chapter 7

P: The Destination

The specific life change after sustained use

Designs Job 3: the destination.

POLE is not a checklist. It is a system where each element increases or decreases the value of the others. Designing one well does not compensate for another done poorly. A strong P with a weak O means the destination exists but nobody believes they can reach it. A strong O with wrong L means you paid to bring someone to a door that is still locked. The four chapters that follow are not sequential steps. They are interdependencies.
"Every weak POLE element shows up somewhere else as effort. Weak O becomes an education problem. Weak L becomes an onboarding problem. Weak E becomes a re-engagement problem. The symptoms look different. The cause is the same."

Every product points somewhere. Most teams never name where.

They describe features, improvements, experiences. All of which are real. None of which answer the question: what is different if this works?

P is not what the product does. P is what the customer's world looks like after it works. If you remove the product from the description and the value remains, you have found P. If the value disappears with the product, you have not.

"If the destination is weak, improvement only makes irrelevance more efficient."

P is not a tagline. It is a design brief: a precise statement of the life change that makes every other product decision evaluable. If P is right, O, L, and E have a target. If P is wrong, they have none.

The cascade: weak P does not just weaken P. It corrupts every element downstream. O cannot break the right belief if the destination is vague. It will break some belief, probably the wrong one. L cannot remove the right barrier if it is unclear what the customer is switching toward. E cannot compound toward the right outcome if the outcome was never defined. You will build a product that feels coherent, converts reasonably, and then slowly empties. Not because execution failed. Because the destination was wrong from the start.
POLE weakest link: quick diagnostic
Symptom: high acquisition, high churn
Symptom: low conversion despite positive feedback
Root cause: weak P or weak E. Users arrive, believe, but have nothing to stay for.
Root cause: weak L. Belief was created. Switching cost was never removed.
Symptom: strong usage, unstable retention
Symptom: slow growth despite deep retention

Three Types of P

Not all north stars work the same way. Before writing P, classify which type your product produces. Each type requires a different E architecture and a different definition of success.

Terminal P requires a different E architecture. If P is delivered once, retention is not the goal: advocacy and re-acquisition at the next cycle are. Every E investment must serve referral or the next instance of the problem, not session-to-session loyalty.

Three Tests for P

  1. Competitor test: can you replace your P with a competitor's name and it still reads correctly? If yes, it is a category description. Raise it until it fails.
  2. Five-year test: will this outcome still matter to your customer in five years? If the problem ages out, P is at the wrong altitude.
  3. Emotion test: does your customer feel something when they read this P? Relief. Recognition. If it produces no emotional reaction, it is too abstract.

Surfacing the obstacle thesis

Before writing P, challenge the assumed mechanism in your market. Every category operates on an unstated assumption about how value is created. The obstacle thesis lives in the gap between that assumption and what the customer actually needs. These three questions surface it:

  1. Write the assumed HOW. "Every product in this market assumed value = [mechanism]."
  2. Challenge it. "What if value is actually [different mechanism]?"
  3. Ask what if that's wrong. Surface the assumed mechanism and test whether your product also assumes it.

Spotify: the category assumed "music = something you find." Spotify challenged that: what if music finds you? That reframe became the obstacle thesis, and it changed P, O, and E simultaneously.

TurboTax: the category assumed better form-filling UX reduces the chore of tax filing. Ethnographic research revealed that for every 2–4 hours spent filing, users spent equal time collecting data. The bottleneck was not the form. It was the data. Real HOW: move the customer from data entry to data verifier. Automate collection. Let users confirm, not type.

O: The Belief Moment

Understandable before first use. Verifiable after first use.

Designs Job 1: belief.

Before a customer uses your product, they make a decision. Not to adopt. Not to pay. To believe.

If that decision is no, nothing else matters.

Teams think belief comes from explanation. So they describe features, list benefits, compare alternatives. They assume clarity creates belief. It doesn't. Customers do not evaluate products from first principles. They use shortcuts. They ask: have I seen something like this work before? Does this look like it will work for someone like me? What would have to be true for this to fail? If those questions are not resolved quickly, they leave.

"If belief does not change in the first session, behaviour will not change in the second."

O is the most underrated contribution in product thinking. Every team can tell you what their product does. Very few can name the one dominant doubt a new customer holds on arrival, and which moment in session 1 removes it. That gap is where most acquisition strategies fail.

O is the moment a new customer first believes that P is possible. It must fire in session 1. If it does not, the customer leaves before E can begin. Everything invested in E (the compounding layer, the moat, the data currency) is unreachable if O never fires. O is the gate everything else passes through.

The tradeoff: if you optimise O without fixing L, you do not increase adoption. You increase curiosity. More people arrive at the door. The same proportion walk away. The acquisition number improves. The conversion number does not move. This is the most common misdiagnosis in growth work: mistaking O performance for adoption performance. O creates belief. L creates adoption. They are different jobs.

O is not just apparent before use. It breaks the specific sceptical belief the obstacle thesis identified. If you cannot trace O back to the obstacle thesis, your O is not designed. It is a default: a feature that happened to create a positive first impression, not an architecture that was built to break a specific barrier.

The one doubt

Every customer arrives with one dominant reason this will not work. Not many. One. If you do not identify it, you will explain everything and convince nothing. The test: ask "what would have to be true for this to fail?" The answer is your belief barrier. That is what O must remove. Not the positioning. Not the messaging. Not the features. The doubt.

Belief comes from evidence, not claims. Evidence can take different forms: demonstration, social proof, visible outcome, immediate experience. But all of them do one thing: they remove the dominant doubt. You do not need to prove everything. You need to remove one doubt. Trying to prove everything creates confusion. Removing one doubt creates belief.

Hard O vs Soft O

  • Hard O: verifiable with a number before first use. "8x cheaper." "1 gigabyte." The number is the proof. No login required.
  • Soft O: experienced in the first 30 seconds before any commitment. Suno: type a description, hear a song. The value is demonstrated, not stated.

One trap with Hard O: over-indexing on the number at the cost of emotional resonance. "8x cheaper" works because it breaks a specific belief about bank fees. A number without a belief it is breaking is a statistic, not an O. The number is the mechanism. Breaking the belief is the point.

"If you can only advertise it, you do not have an O. You have a claim."

Three O Failure Modes

O fires too late

The belief-breaking moment exists but takes too long to arrive. Day 7 retention is low even when Month 3 is strong. Users explore and leave before O fires.

Fix: move O to session 1. If it cannot get there, redesign the first-session experience around it.
O fires for the wrong segment

O was designed for early adopters but the product is now acquiring a different segment. The belief being broken is not the belief this segment holds.

Fix: name the belief the current acquisition segment holds on arrival. Redesign O to break exactly that belief.
O never fires

No belief-breaking moment exists in session 1. Users explore the product and leave without ever experiencing the moment that would make P feel possible.

Fix: name the specific sceptical belief. Design one session 1 moment that breaks it. Test with five recent sign-ups: "Was there a moment where you thought this might actually work?"

L: Liberation Design

Removes the old barrier. Enables adoption without rebuild.

Designs Job 2: switching.

Note on naming: BELT uses L to name the lock-in type, the specific barrier keeping the segment out. POLE uses L for Liberation, the deliberate removal of that barrier. Same letter, two roles: one diagnoses, one designs. The BELT diagnosis tells you which liberation to build.

By the time a customer reaches your product, they have already decided not to use it. Not consciously. Not explicitly. But correctly. They have a specific reason. If that reason is still true, they will not switch.

BELT's L asked: why have they not already switched? POLE's L answers: what must be removed for them to switch now? If these two do not match, nothing will move.

O created the belief that P is possible. L picks up where O stops. O breaks a belief. L removes the structural cost of acting on that belief. A customer can believe the product works and still not adopt it, because switching requires protecting something they have already invested in. L removes that cost precisely.

Liberation is not guidance. It is removal. It removes the exact cost that kept the customer in their current state. Not gradually. Not eventually. Immediately. Generic ease of use is not L.

The tradeoff: strong L without O removes the barrier before the customer believes crossing it is worth anything. You have made switching easy for someone who has not yet decided to switch. The friction is gone. The conviction is not there. This produces trials that do not convert, not because the product is bad, but because belief was not established before the barrier was removed. O and L must work in sequence or in convergence. L alone is a door with no reason to open it.

E: The Retention Arc

The architecture that makes leaving increasingly costly

Designs Job 4: compounding value.

P defined where the customer is going. O gave them a reason to believe they could get there. L removed the barrier to starting. E is what O and L were always leading toward: the architecture that ensures each session deposits value that did not exist before, until leaving means losing something irreplaceable.

"Retention is not a feature. It is a consequence of accumulated value."

A customer uses your product once. Then again. Then stops. Nothing broke. Nothing failed. Nothing was lost. That is the problem.

The tradeoff: strong E with wrong P is a moat around nothing. You have built a system that compounds, that makes leaving costly, that creates genuine data accumulation, in service of a destination the customer never cared about enough. The retention numbers look reasonable. The renewal conversations are hard. The NPS is mediocre. Because the customer is locked in, not delighted. Lock-in without outcome is a tax, not a moat. Fix P before investing in E.

Teams treat retention as a behaviour problem. They try to increase engagement, send reminders, build habits. They assume that if users return, retention will follow. It doesn't. Retention is not caused by usage. It is caused by value that remains. If nothing accumulates, each session starts from zero. And what starts from zero can be abandoned without loss.

"What does not accumulate cannot retain. It can only remind."

E is the architecture that changes that equation: each session must deposit value that did not exist before, until leaving means losing something irreplaceable.

A relationship with a writer whose thinking you now understand deeply. The hundredth issue lands differently from the first. The archive and relationship are irreplaceable. Switching is not changing the source. It is starting over.
Critical dependency: most teams invest in E-Accrued before E-Delivered is working. The moat forms over a gap that churns customers before they reach it. Fix E-Delivered first. Then layer E-Accrued on a working foundation.

The 5-Step E Design Ladder

1
Decompose P into components
What specific things must be true in each session for P-state to hold? If P is "music finds you before you know you want it," session components include: recommendations feel surprising but right, taste model improves, no effort required.
2
Set the Functional value proposition floor
Minimum threshold per component. Below this threshold, P-state resets to zero. Spotify: if cross-device sync fails, no amount of Discover Weekly quality saves the session.
3
Derive Experience value proposition from the obstacle thesis
Which dimension (Speed, Relevance, Clarity, Control) is load-bearing? This is the dimension the obstacle thesis named. It must be the dimension E-Delivered optimises for.
4
Identify the E-Accrued layer
What does the user build inside the product that cannot be taken with them? E-Accrued takes four forms:

Data: the product becomes more useful because it knows more. Spotify taste model, financial history, fitness tracking. Leaving means losing insight the product built about you.

Capability: the customer becomes better through using the product. Grammarly style model, design tool component libraries, coding environments that learn patterns. Leaving means losing the leverage the product built for you.

Structure: the product becomes embedded in how work or life happens. Notion workspace, team workflows, systems of record. Leaving means disruption, not just inconvenience.

Social: the product becomes tied to community membership, professional identity, or status. Leaving means losing relationships and standing, not just data.

Strava: segment leaderboards, Strava-specific PRs, a follower network built around athletic identity. Switching to Garmin means losing the social layer, not just the data. Figma: shared component libraries, design system collaboration, a professional identity as a "Figma designer", not just a designer who uses Figma. The shared creative culture is a moat as real as the component library. LinkedIn: professional network, connection history, endorsements, a profile that represents career identity accumulated over years. Leaving LinkedIn is not switching tools. It is rebuilding a professional presence.

This is the most underestimated form of E-Accrued in utility products. Teams dismiss it as an entertainment moat (social = engagement = TikTok). It is not. When social accumulation is tied to professional identity or community standing, it operates as structural switching cost in an otherwise utility product. Design for it deliberately, or discover it too late.

Identify which form (or forms) your product accumulates. Each has different implications for switching cost and moat design.
5
E-P Alignment: cut features that don't trace to P
For every feature: does it trace to Step 1 via the obstacle thesis in two steps or fewer? If not, cut it. It is consuming roadmap without advancing P-state.

The Referral Moment

Referral is not a feature. It is the moment E-Accrued becomes perceivable and shareable. Spotify Wrapped works because it fires in December, after 12 months of E-Accrued listening history. The value was already there. Wrapped made it visible. Triggering referral at adoption (Stage 3) before E-Accrued has formed produces an empty chart and no sharing.

The rule: referral fires when P is realised and E-Accrued is perceivable. Not before.

Non-obvious truth: most PMs optimise the acquisition layer while the retention layer collapses underneath them. Strong O. Weak E. A leaky bucket with a very good tap. Fix E-Delivered first. Then E-Accrued. Then referral.

Value Currency

Time, Data, Money

Every product creates value. Very few capture it well. Not because the product is weak. Because the form of value is misunderstood.

Value is not abstract. It always takes a form. And every product is an exchange: the customer gives something, and the product returns something. If that exchange is unclear, conversion drops, trust weakens, and monetisation stalls, even if the product is genuinely useful.

Every product accumulates at least one currency. The moat only forms when the customer can feel what they have built. If the moat is invisible, it does not protect.

Part Four — Value Lifecycle  |  Chapter 12

The Value Lifecycle

Nine stages. Three actors. Design for all of them.

A product does not deliver value once. It delivers value across a sequence. Each step creates value, transfers value, or loses value. Most teams optimise individual steps. Very few see the system.

The failure is rarely within a stage. It happens between them. Expectation set, then not met. Belief created, then not reinforced. Value shown, then not sustained. The product works. The system does not. Most breakdowns do not happen within a stage. They happen in the handoff.

Value does not stop at delivery. It must be promised, perceived, delivered, received, realised, reinforced, and captured to complete the cycle. Most products design for Stages 1–4 and leave everything else to chance.

Part Five — Diagnostics  |  Chapter 13

The Weakest Link Audit

One weak element caps everything else

At any point in time, one part of your system is failing more than the rest. Not two. Not three. One. Everything else is secondary.

Teams try to improve everything. They build roadmaps with better onboarding, more features, improved retention, increased acquisition. Each makes sense. Together, they dilute focus. Because not all problems matter equally. A system does not fail everywhere. It fails where it is weakest. And that weakness defines the outcome.

Improving a strong part does not fix a weak one. It hides it. When metrics improve in one area but overall performance does not change, the system is telling you: you are fixing the wrong thing.

POLE elements compound each other. A strong E cannot compensate for a weak P. The weakest link determines the ceiling of the entire product.

Weakest link: find it before you fix it

Check O first: is belief forming in session 1? Then check L: is the switching barrier removed?

Everything looks reasonable. Nothing is growing.
Check E: is the moat visible? Is the customer being shown what they would lose? Stage 8 gap.
Check P: is the destination specific enough? A vague P produces mediocre everything. Start here.

PropertyGuru: a full BELT+POLE walkthrough

What the founding thesis got right, what it missed, and what the framework surfaces

I spent several years at PropertyGuru, one of Southeast Asia's largest property platforms. This case is different from the others in this book — I am not diagnosing from the outside. I am showing you what the framework would have surfaced had we run it explicitly at the time, and what it explains about the decisions that followed.

The founding thesis

PropertyGuru was built on a clear and correct observation: finding property in Southeast Asia was painful. Listings were fragmented across newspapers, agents, and word of mouth. The thesis: aggregate listings in one place, make search frictionless, connect buyers to agents. The problem was real. The execution was strong. Adoption came.

And then a subtler problem emerged. Traffic grew. Listings grew. But the depth of relationship with the buyer — the most valuable user — did not compound the way it should have for a platform of that scale. Buyers came, searched, transacted, and left. The platform was a utility for the transaction, not a system for the decision.

All surface features that confirm the platform has inventory.The belief that needed breaking was: "I cannot know if I am paying the right price in this market." Hard O: show the buyer what similar properties transacted at — not listed at — in the last 90 days. Verifiable before any inquiry. That breaks the dominant doubt.
L: LiberationEasy search UX. Filter by price, size, location. Designed for Inertia (first step is easy) but the actual barrier for the serious buyer was Search Cost (evaluation uncertainty). Wrong lock-in type. Wrong liberation design.Show outcome before commitment. Let the buyer see the full market picture — price trends, transaction history, agent reputation scores — before making a single inquiry. Reverse trial for a financial decision.
E: Retention arcSaved searches. Email alerts for new listings. Functional but no accumulation. Each new search cycle starts from roughly the same baseline. The platform did not learn the buyer.E-Accrued built on Decision Data: the buyer's search history, shortlists, price sensitivity, neighbourhood comparisons, and life stage context. After 6 months of serious searching, the platform should know this buyer better than any agent. Leaving means losing a decision profile that took months to build.

The outcome. PropertyGuru's subsequent product investments — transaction data products, agent reputation systems, valuation tools, and market intelligence features — validated this diagnosis directionally. The platform was moving toward the Enabling buyer, the data-rich decision product, and away from pure listing aggregation. The BELT+POLE framework would have named that direction explicitly earlier, before competitive pressure from 99.co made the strategic necessity visible through the metrics.

The lesson is not that PropertyGuru made wrong decisions. It is that BELT+POLE makes the right direction legible before the data forces it. The founding thesis was correct about B. It underspecified E, misidentified L for the buyer segment, and classified T as Transient when the most valuable segment had an Enabling problem. Every subsequent strategic shift addressed exactly those gaps.

"The founding thesis gets you to traction. BELT+POLE gets you to the architecture that sustains it."

Duolingo

Strong O. Strong engagement. Weak P. The difference between learning a language and practising an app.

"Duolingo built one of the most effective engagement systems in consumer software. The question the framework forces is whether engagement and the outcome the customer actually needs are the same thing."

The obstacle thesis

The category assumed: language learning fails because it is inaccessible, expensive, and boring. Rosetta Stone charged $200–500 for software. Babbel charged $13 per month. Classroom instruction required scheduling and commitment. The assumed mechanism: make it affordable, accessible, and fun — and people will learn.

Duolingo challenged the mechanism. What if the barrier is not cost or access but consistency? Most people who buy Rosetta Stone never finish it. Most people who start language classes drop out. The real obstacle thesis: language learning fails because people cannot maintain the habit long enough for acquisition to occur. The founding insight was behavioural, not economic.

Running BELT

BELTWhat it surfaces
B: BehaviourThe workaround for serious language learners was a combination of expensive software, YouTube videos, conversation exchange apps, and occasional classes. Elaborate, inconsistent, and abandoned repeatedly. The bright spot was not people who finished Rosetta Stone — it was people who found ways to make practice daily: labelling household objects, watching TV with subtitles, finding a language exchange partner. The behaviour that persisted was always low-friction and daily, not intensive and periodic.
E: Enduring outcomeTwo distinct enduring outcomes exist in the same product. The first: functional fluency in a second language — the ability to navigate a country, hold a conversation, read a menu. This is real, deeply enduring, and worth years of effort. The second: the feeling of making daily progress toward that goal. Duolingo is built around the second. The first is implied but structurally unguaranteed.
L: Lock-in typeInertia, with a specific flavour: identity inertia. "I am not the kind of person who sticks to language learning." The barrier is not cost (Duolingo is free), not access (it is on every device), and not prior investment in a competitor. It is the accumulated evidence of previous failure. L must break the belief that this time will be different — not through persuasion, but through a first experience so frictionless that failure feels structurally impossible.
T: TypeEnabling — but only if the outcome is genuine language acquisition. Each session should deposit something that makes the next session more valuable: vocabulary retained, grammar internalised, patterns recognised. The question BELT forces: does the Duolingo experience actually compound toward fluency? Or does it compound toward Duolingo proficiency — the ability to complete Duolingo exercises — which is not the same thing?

POLE diagnosis

POLE elementCurrent designWhat the obstacle thesis demands
P: Destination"Learn a language for free." Feature-level P. Applies to every language app in every market. The destination is the mechanism, not the life change."For adults who have tried and quit before, Duolingo delivers the daily habit that makes a second language feel possible — so the next trip, the next conversation, the next job feels different." Specific segment, specific life change, passes the product removal test.
O: Belief momentStrong. One lesson in three minutes. Immediate, verifiable, low-commitment. The belief formed: "I can do this daily." This is a well-designed Soft O that fires in the first session. Duolingo's O is genuinely one of the best in consumer software.The obstacle thesis demands O breaks the identity barrier: "I am not the kind of person who sticks to this." The streak mechanic does exactly that — it makes quitting visible and makes consistency feel like identity. This is the right design for Inertia lock-in.
L: LiberationNear-perfect for Inertia. Free. No account required to start. First lesson in 60 seconds. No goal-setting before value. The product removes every first-step barrier before asking for anything.Correct lock-in diagnosis, correct liberation design. This is Duolingo's strongest POLE element and explains rapid adoption at scale.
E: Retention arcE-Delivered is strong: well-designed exercises, immediate feedback, variable reward. E-Accrued is the gap. The streak is visible but what it represents — days practised, not language acquired — is the wrong currency. After 365 days of Duolingo, what has the user built that they cannot take elsewhere? Their streak. Not their fluency. Not a transferable language asset. The moat is platform-specific, not outcome-specific.E-Accrued should compound toward the enduring outcome: demonstrable fluency progress that the user can feel and measure. A personal vocabulary model, conversation complexity over time, pronunciation improvement. None of this currently exists in a form the user can perceive. Stage 8 is absent.

The verdict. Duolingo is a masterclass in O and L design and one of the weakest examples of P and E design in consumer software. The engagement metrics are extraordinary. The outcome delivery is structurally ambiguous. The obstacle thesis — language learning fails because people cannot maintain the habit — was answered correctly. But the enduring outcome was never precisely defined, which meant E was built to sustain the habit rather than to compound toward the fluency the habit was supposed to produce.

The result is a product with 500 million downloads, extraordinary daily active users, and a widely held suspicion among serious language learners that it does not actually teach you a language. Both things are true. Strong O + strong L + weak P + weak E-Accrued produces exactly that outcome: massive adoption, habitual use, and an enduring question about whether the destination was ever real.

"Duolingo solved the habit problem. It never fully committed to the fluency problem. Those are not the same product."

Linear

The founding thesis as an explicit BELT diagnosis. What happens when a product is built by people who ran the framework without naming it.

"Linear did not improve issue tracking. It changed who the product was for. That is a segmentation decision, not a feature decision."

The obstacle thesis

Jira dominated software project management for twenty years. Linear's founding team had used Jira. Their obstacle thesis was precise: Jira was built for project managers managing engineers. The category assumed that the buyer and the user were the same person, or that the buyer's needs should determine the product. Linear challenged that assumption directly. What if the product was built for the engineer, not the project manager? What if speed and simplicity were not trade-offs against power, but the entire point?

The obstacle thesis: engineering teams are not slow because their work is complex. They are slow because their tools are built for the people who manage them, not the people who do the work. Every Jira feature that makes a project manager's life easier makes an engineer's life slightly worse. That accumulation of friction was the barrier Linear was built to remove.

Running BELT

BELTWhat it surfaces
B: BehaviourThe workaround was universal among engineering teams who used Jira: shadow systems. Engineers maintained their own Notion pages, Slack threads, and personal to-do lists alongside Jira because Jira was where work was tracked, not where work was done. The bright spot was the engineer who had found a way to minimise their Jira surface area — who used it only when forced to, and built everything else elsewhere. That workaround described the product Linear needed to be.
E: Enduring outcomeFor the engineering team segment: shipping without the overhead of managing the tool that is supposed to help you ship. The enduring outcome is not "organised issues." It is the feeling that the tool disappears into the work rather than adding to it. After two years of Linear, an engineering team should be faster and more coordinated — not more proficient at issue tracking.
L: Lock-in typeSwitching Cost, with significant organisational friction. Jira holds years of issue history, sprint data, project structure, and integrations. Leaving requires migration of data, re-establishing workflows, and — most importantly — getting organisational buy-in to switch a tool that the people who approved the budget (project managers and engineering managers) are often most comfortable with. Linear's liberation had to address not just the data migration but the internal political cost of switching.
T: TypeEnabling. Each sprint, each issue, each retrospective deposits data that compounds into a picture of how the team works: velocity patterns, recurring blockers, individual and team throughput. The product should become more accurate at predicting what the team can ship as it learns how that specific team operates. The moat is team-specific operational intelligence, not generic project data.

POLE diagnosis

POLE elementLinear's designAssessment
P: Destination"The issue tracker built for high-performance teams." Slightly above feature level — "high-performance" implies an outcome. But it still describes a tool category, not a life change.Closer than most. The real P: "For engineering teams drowning in process overhead, Linear delivers the experience of shipping fast without the tool getting in the way." The founding thesis is P-level thinking. The marketing has not always matched it.
O: Belief momentSpeed. Linear's first-session experience is designed to feel fast — keyboard shortcuts, instant response, minimal loading states. The belief formed: "This is what issue tracking should feel like." It fires in the first five minutes without any data migration or setup.Strong Soft O. Correctly identifies that the dominant doubt is "can a tool actually feel this different?" and answers it immediately through experience rather than claims. The belief is formed before any commitment is required.
L: LiberationFree tier with full functionality. Import from Jira. Teams can run Linear alongside Jira before committing. The political problem — getting organisational approval to switch — is addressed by making the parallel run cost nothing.Correctly identifies Switching Cost as the lock-in type. The import tool is the right liberation design. The political cost (manager buy-in) is partially addressed through the free tier but remains the hardest barrier — Linear is still adopted bottom-up, which means it requires a champion willing to fight for the switch.
E: Retention arcE-Delivered is strong: the experience compounds with team adoption. E-Accrued builds through issue history, cycle data, and team velocity patterns. The product genuinely knows your team after six months of use in a way a new Jira instance does not.The moat is real but not yet visible enough. Stage 8 is the gap: Linear does not currently show the team what they would lose — the operational intelligence, the velocity baseline, the friction reduction data — before they consider alternatives. The switching cost is high. The customer cannot feel it.

The verdict. Linear is the clearest example in this book of a product built on a correct obstacle thesis. The founding team ran an implicit BELT diagnosis — they named the workaround (shadow systems), identified the enduring outcome (shipping without overhead), classified the lock-in type (Switching Cost with organisational friction), and correctly classified the product as Enabling. The result is a product with unusually strong product-market fit in its target segment.

The remaining gap is Stage 8. Linear's moat is real — team velocity data, issue patterns, operational intelligence accumulated over years. The customer does not see it until they consider leaving, which is exactly the wrong moment to discover it. One well-designed reinforcement moment per quarter — "your team closed 847 issues this year, your average cycle time dropped 23%, here is what your team has built in Linear" — would make the switching cost felt rather than merely structural.

"Linear built the right product for the right segment with the right obstacle thesis. The moat is real. The customer just cannot see it yet."

The pattern across all cases

Every product is strong somewhere and failing somewhere. The interaction between the strong element and the weak one determines the outcome.

PatternWhat it producesExample
Strong O, weak PHigh acquisition, high churn. Users try and leave because nothing was worth staying for.Robinhood: frictionless entry, no confidence destination.
Strong P, weak LAdmiration without adoption. People understand and want the outcome but do not switch.Any product with strong vision but misidentified switching barrier.
Strong usage, weak EActivity without value. Users return but nothing accumulates, leaving is costless.Calm: daily sessions, no E-Accrued layer.
Strong E, weak ODeep retention among believers, slow growth. Hard to convince new customers to start.Notion early: powerful once inside, hard to believe before.

Use these patterns diagnostically. When a product is underperforming, identify the strong element first, then find what it cannot compensate for. That is the weakest link.

Run the Diagnostic in 30 Minutes

Five steps. One output. Applied to any product you know.

This is not a workshop. It is a filter. The goal is not to be complete. The goal is to be correct enough to avoid building the wrong thing.

Do this alone or with your team. Do not debate definitions. Answer what is true. Each step has a stopping condition. If you hit one, stop. You are not ready to design.

1
B: Find the behaviour (5 min)
Write down the specific workaround your most committed early customers used before your product existed. Make it concrete: tool, steps, repetition, friction. "They used spreadsheets" is not specific enough.

Stopping condition: if you cannot describe the workaround concretely, stop. You have a hypothesis, not confirmed demand. Do not proceed until B is clear.
2
E: Name the life change (5 min)
Complete: "For [specific segment], [product] delivers [specific outcome], so [life is different in this specific way]." Apply the product removal test: remove the product: does the value remain? Run So What? until the answer stops mentioning the product.

Stopping condition: if you are building features, not defining a change. If the outcome still mentions the product, rewrite.
3
L: Identify the lock-in type (5 min)
Which one describes your segment: Switching Cost, Search Cost, or Inertia? Write the single dominant reason your segment has not already switched. Classify it precisely.

Stopping condition: if you cannot name the barrier precisely, or if your onboarding describes generic setup rather than removing the specific barrier you named: stop. You are designing adoption, not hope.
4
T: Validate the type (5 min)
Ask: does solving this problem make the next use more valuable, or does it reset to zero? Does the 601st session feel different from the first?

If Enabling: design for accumulation from day one. If Transient: do not invest in retention systems expecting compounding. Compete on speed, convenience, and reliability instead.

Stopping condition: if T is Transient and you are planning a compounding E architecture: stop. The ceiling has been set.
5
Score your POLE (10 min)
Score each element H/M/L on Clarity and Credibility. The lowest combined score is your Weakest Link. That is your only priority. Fix it before investing in any other element.
