Foreword

The Trap Nobody Talks About

You are probably solving the wrong type of problem

There is a specific way product teams fail that nobody in the industry talks about clearly. They solve the wrong type of problem. Not a bad problem. Not a fake problem. The wrong type. And the distinction is everything.

A transient problem goes away on its own or after first use. The customer needed something once. You delivered it. They left. That is not a product failure. That is the natural consequence of solving a transient problem. But most product teams treat that churn as a retention problem and spend years trying to fix it with features, notifications, and loyalty mechanics. None of it works. You cannot retain someone who never had a reason to stay.

An enduring problem persists. It recurs. The customer needs a solution every week, every month, for years. This is the only type of problem that can sustain a product. And it is rarer than most teams think.

"If your product is solving a transient problem, you do not have a retention problem. You have a diagnosis problem. No feature will fix it."

This book is a system for getting that diagnosis right before you design anything. It has two parts. BELT is the diagnostic engine: four questions that force you to classify the problem type before any design decision is made. POLE is the design architecture: four value propositions, each doing a different job at a different moment in the customer journey.

The sequence is non-negotiable. BELT before POLE. Diagnosis before design. The quality of everything you build in POLE is entirely determined by the quality of your BELT diagnosis. Skip the diagnosis and you design confidently toward the wrong destination.

How to Read This Book

Do not skip BELT to get to POLE. The sequence is the point. Do not skip the Introduction — the utility vs entertainment distinction is the first gate, not optional context. Read at least one case study in Part Six before running the framework on your own product. The 30-minute application guide at the end is where this becomes operational.

Introduction

Where This Framework Applies

Utility products, entertainment products, and the difference that determines everything

Before running any BELT diagnostic, answer one question: is this a utility product or an entertainment product? The answer determines whether the framework applies, how it applies, and where its limits are.

The Core Distinction

A utility product solves a specific, recurring problem. The customer uses it because it moves them toward a defined outcome they would pursue even without the product. The product is instrumental. Value is measured in progress.

An entertainment product delivers hedonic value. The customer uses it because the experience itself is enjoyable. There is no destination. There is no problem being solved. Value is measured in engagement, not progress.

The Test

Ask a loyal customer: "What specific outcome does this product help you achieve?" A utility customer can answer this. An entertainment customer gives you consumption language: "It's entertaining," "I enjoy it," "I always have something to watch." Neither is wrong — but only one is the BELT+POLE customer.

Utility productEntertainment product
Why the customer returnsProgress toward a specific outcome. The product is instrumental.The experience itself is enjoyable. Variable reward loops, content freshness, social validation.
What retention looks likeCustomer can describe what they are building.Customer describes consumption. Time passes pleasurably.
What the moat isData currency: irreplaceable personal asset built through use.Content library, social graph, or algorithmic feed quality.
BELT+POLE relevanceHigh. All four diagnostic questions are meaningful.Low. B is weak. E is undefined. L is social, not data.

The Spectrum

Applies fullyApplies partiallyDoes not apply
Grammarly, TurboTax, Notion, Strava, Wise, Superhuman, RipplingSpotify (utility segment), Airbnb, Uber, LinkedIn, KindleNetflix, TikTok, Instagram (consumption mode)

The Entertainment Trap

Many product teams build utility products but measure them like entertainment products — maximising session length, daily active users, and content consumption. When utility products optimise for engagement rather than outcome progress, they design features that move a metric without advancing P-state. Calm's streak is the most cited example: it optimises sessions opened, not felt calm. The metric is entertainment-native. The product is utility.

Four Diagnostic Questions Before You Begin

  1. Can a loyal customer name a specific outcome? Not "I enjoy it" — a specific destination. If yes: utility framing is valid.
  2. Is there a workaround? Does the bright-spot customer do something manual, effortful, and specific to solve this problem without your product? If yes: utility signal.
  3. Does the hundredth use deliver more value than the first? If yes: Enabling. If no: closer to entertainment.
  4. What would a customer lose by stopping? Utility: something irreplaceable they built. Entertainment: they would miss it, but nothing is lost.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 1

The Diagnosis That Changes Everything

Most product failures are not execution failures. They are diagnosis failures.

In 2013, Robinhood launched with a founding thesis that would shape every product decision for the next decade: young adults are not investing because commissions are too high. Remove the commission. The market opens up. Problem solved.

The thesis was plausible. It was logical. It was wrong.

Young adults were not staying out of financial markets because of $9.99 commissions. They were staying out because they did not know what to buy and were terrified of making the wrong decision. The barrier was financial confidence, not financial access. These are different problems. They require different products.

"When the obstacle thesis is wrong, every feature built on it is wrong — regardless of execution quality. The wrong diagnosis is not a small mistake. It is the entire architecture."

BELT: Four Questions That Force the Right Diagnosis

QuestionWhat it surfacesWhat it kills
B — What are they doing despite friction?The workaround. Proof that the problem is real and unsolved.Hypothetical customers. Problems that exist only in interviews.
E — What outcome do they actually need?The life change. The destination, not the feature.Feature-level P. Products that optimise the mechanism instead of the outcome.
L — Why have they not switched?The specific barrier. What liberation must remove.Generic onboarding. Liberation designed for the wrong lock-in type.
T — Transient or Enabling?Whether depth value can form. Whether E architecture is possible.E investment on Transient products. Moats that can never form.

Chapter Summary

Robinhood: wrong obstacle thesis → wrong E architecture → wrong features. Regardless of execution quality.

Most product failures are not execution failures. They are diagnosis failures. The team was answering the wrong question brilliantly.

BELT forces four questions in sequence. Each one is designed to surface the assumption most likely to be wrong. The sequence is non-negotiable.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 2

B — Behaviour

What are they doing despite friction?

The most reliable signal of real demand is not a survey. It is a person who has already built their own solution to the problem you are trying to solve.

The Bright Spot Method

Before any product exists, some customers solve the problem themselves. They log food in Notes apps. They record Loom-like videos in Zoom and share the link. They build finance spreadsheets that track their portfolio in ways apps do not. These are bright spots.

B asks: "What are they doing despite the friction of doing it manually?" The answer defines your most valuable early customer.

Two Bright Spots in Practice

ProductThe workaround (the bright spot)What it confirms
Calendly Professionals sending chains of 5–8 emails to schedule a single meeting. "Are you free Tuesday?" "Tuesday is bad, how about Thursday?" "Thursday works — morning or afternoon?" The workaround is elaborate, universal, and embarrassing enough that people joke about it. The scheduling pain is real and the market is enormous. Nobody does this because they enjoy it. They do it because there is no obvious, frictionless alternative they trust.
Notion Teams managing knowledge across a patchwork of Google Docs, Slack threads, email chains, and personal spreadsheets — each person maintaining their own system, none of it connected. When someone new joins, they spend two weeks asking "where is the process doc for X?" and getting a different answer each time. The knowledge management pain is real and structural. Teams are not doing this by choice. They are doing it because nothing has made the alternative feel worth the switch.

The Bright Spot Interview — Three Questions

  1. "Walk me through the last time you dealt with [the problem]. What did you actually do?"
  2. "What tools or workarounds are you currently using?" (Never ask if they have a workaround — ask what their workaround is.)
  3. "What would you lose if you had to stop using that workaround tomorrow?" This is the most important question. The answer reveals the E architecture in embryonic form.

The FUDU Score

Score the intensity of the problem using four dimensions. Each scores 1–3. The FUDU score tells you whether the problem is worth building for.

Before scoring, answer one gate question: Is this problem uniquely underserved? If the market already has good solutions, a high FUDU score builds a product that competes on execution, not architecture. Name the gap first. Then score the intensity.

F Frequency
How often does this pain recur?
Does the problem come back regardless of prior attempts to solve it?
1 = monthly  |  2 = weekly  |  3 = daily
U Ubiquity
How many people face this problem?
Is this niche, broad professional, or universal?
1 = niche  |  2 = broad professional  |  3 = universal
D Depth
How much does it hurt when it occurs?
Is the pain merely inconvenient, or does it block the customer from proceeding?
1 = inconvenient  |  2 = frustrating  |  3 = blocking
U Urgency
How pressing is the need to act right now?
Can the customer defer resolution, or must they act immediately?
1 = deferrable  |  2 = pressing  |  3 = must resolve now

FUDU summary for Calendly: Frequency: HIGH (multiple times per day). Ubiquity: HIGH (every professional who schedules meetings). Depth: HIGH (15–30 minutes wasted, credibility cost). Urgency: HIGH (the thread is live, the meeting is this week — cannot be deferred).

All four dimensions high. Worth building for.

Products with combined FUDU below 9 rarely sustain motivation long enough for E to compound. Above 18 across all four dimensions: structural demand. The problem is real, broad, painful, and urgent.

Exercise: Finding Your Bright Spots

  1. Describe the specific workaround your most valuable early customers were using before your product existed. Make it concrete — "they used spreadsheets" is not specific enough.
  2. Gate question: is this problem uniquely underserved? Who else is trying to solve it and how well?
  3. Score the problem on FUDU (1–3 each). Which dimensions are highest? Which is the weakest signal?
  4. What would those customers lose if they had to abandon your product tomorrow? Is that loss the moat you designed — or a happy accident?

Chapter Summary

Bright spots are customers who solved the problem with a workaround. Most reliable signal of real demand.

Three interview questions. The third ("what would you lose?") is the most important.

FUDU gate: before scoring, confirm the problem is uniquely underserved. Then score Frequency × Ubiquity × Depth × Urgency.

Non-obvious truth: user feedback over-indexes on transient pain. The problem that generates the loudest complaints is almost never the enduring problem worth solving. Listen to what customers do, not what they say.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 3

E — Enduring

What outcome do they actually need?

Most products are designed at feature level. The Enduring question forces the team to name the specific life change the customer is trying to produce. Everything downstream depends on getting this right.

Three Levels of Problem Definition

LevelDescriptionExample (fitness)
FeatureWhat the product does."Log your calories."
OutcomeWhat the customer achieves."Lose 10kg in 12 weeks."
Life ChangeHow the customer's life is different."Understand your body so tracking becomes unnecessary."

The So What? Technique

Most teams describe features when asked about P. The So What? technique forces escalation from feature level to life change level. Keep asking until the answer stops mentioning the product.

Loom: from feature claim to PLevel
"One video is worth a thousand words."Feature claim
So what? → "You can explain complex things without scheduling a meeting."Still about the product
So what? → "You can communicate decisions and context to anyone, anytime, asynchronously."Getting closer
So what? → "Your team operates at full speed regardless of time zone or availability."P — answer stops mentioning Loom

The rule: when the so what answer stops mentioning the product, you have found P.

P Statement Template

"For [specific segment], [product] delivers [specific outcome], so [life is different in this specific way]."

Test: could this appear in a feature list? If yes, rewrite at Life Change level.

ProductWrong P (Feature level)Right P (Life Change level)
MyFitnessPal"Track your calories.""For adults who have tried and failed through willpower alone, MFP delivers a complete understanding of their body — so they know what to eat without having to think about it."
Strava"Track your runs and rides.""For endurance athletes who train consistently, Strava delivers visible proof that fitness is compounding — so effort has meaning beyond completion."
Robinhood"Commission-free trading.""For young adults building financial confidence, Robinhood delivers a first real investment from knowledge, not impulse."

Exercise: Writing Your P Statement

  1. Write your current P statement exactly as it appears in your documentation.
  2. Apply the three tests: Feature list? Specific segment? Life change?
  3. Run the So What? technique three times. Does the answer stop mentioning the product?
  4. Rewrite using the template: "For [segment], [product] delivers [outcome], so [life changes in this specific way]."
  5. Show both versions to a customer. Which would they tell a friend?

Chapter Summary

Three levels: Feature, Outcome, Life Change. P must be at Life Change level.

So What? technique: keep asking until the answer stops mentioning the product. That is P.

If P could appear in a feature list, it is at the wrong altitude. Enduring vs Transient: not all problems sustain motivation. E architecture only works for enduring problems.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 4

L — Lock-in

Why have they not switched?

Every customer who has not yet adopted your product is paying some cost to stay where they are. L names that cost and removes it.

Three Lock-in Types

SC
Switching Cost
Prior investment in a competitor. History, data, integrations. Fix: migration. Import. Prove nothing is lost.
SC
Search Cost
Trapped by uncertainty. Evaluation anxiety, not migration. Fix: preview. Free trial. Show outcome before commitment.
I
Inertia
Never started. Belief barrier. "I am not the kind of person..." Fix: zero-commitment first step. No goal required to start.

One Product Per Type

TypeProductWhat the customer has already investedWhat liberation must do
Switching Cost Figma A design team has spent months building component libraries and design systems. Every new component deepens the investment. The cost of leaving is rebuilding months of work from scratch. Figma imports files directly. Nothing is lost. The team starts alongside existing tools.
Search Cost Warby Parker The customer is trapped by evaluation anxiety: will these frames look right on my face? Ordering online without trying first has always felt like a gamble. Warby Parker: five frames, five days, free. Try at home. No commitment until you have seen them on your face.
Inertia Calendly Email scheduling works — badly. There is no crisis. The status quo wins by default because nothing makes the switch feel worth it. Calendly: send one link. First use takes 30 seconds. The switch costs less than the next email thread it replaces.
The wrong liberation is worse than no liberation. Lock-in type determines liberation design. Get the type wrong and the barrier stays intact.
Lock-in typeWhat L must doWhat L must not do
Switching CostMake migration effortless. Import history. Prove nothing is lost."Easy to get started." That is not the barrier.
Search CostShow the outcome before commitment. Remove evaluation risk.Migration tools. There is nothing to migrate.
InertiaReduce the first step to near-zero. No commitment before value.Onboarding requiring belief, goals, or commitment.

Exercise: Diagnosing Your Lock-in Type

  1. Which lock-in type describes your segment: Switching Cost, Search Cost, or Inertia?
  2. What does your current onboarding do to address this specific barrier?
  3. Identify your O+L relationship type: Convergence, Near-convergence, Sequential, or Independent.
  4. Ask five non-adopters why they have not switched. Does their answer confirm your diagnosis?
Non-obvious truth: Lock-in matters more than delight in low-frequency products. A customer who loves your product but has no lock-in will leave the moment a competitor offers a better first session. A customer who is locked in will stay even when they are frustrated.
Part One — BELT  |  Chapter 5

T — Type

Transient or Enabling?

T is the most consequential question in the diagnostic. The answer determines whether an E architecture is possible at all.

TransientEnabling
The hundredth use delivers the same value as the first.The hundredth use is more valuable than the first.
No data accumulates. No moat forms.Personal data, history, or patterns accumulate with each use.
Retention relies on P-Salience, not depth value.Retention compounds. Leaving means losing something irreplaceable.
Wise for a tourist: one transfer, no history.Wise for an expat: 24 months of multi-currency infrastructure.

The T Test

"If a customer stopped using the product for six months and came back, would the product know them? Would the 601st session feel different from the first?" If yes: Enabling. If no: Transient.

Chapter Summary

Enabling: the hundredth use is more valuable than the first. Transient: every session delivers the same value.

Building E architecture on a Transient product is wasted investment.

Non-obvious truth: if your product is solving a transient problem, you do not have a retention problem. You have a diagnosis problem. No feature, no onboarding improvement, and no re-engagement campaign will fix it. The only fix is a different problem.

"Two customers are in the same segment if they share the same enduring pain and the same lock-in type."

Same product, different segments, different designs. If BELT answers diverge within your candidate segment, you have two segments. Split before designing.
Part Two — POLE  |  Chapter 7

P — The Destination

The specific life change after sustained use

P is not a tagline. It is the specific outcome the customer experiences after sustained use. It is the destination that every other POLE element is moving the customer toward.

Three Types of P

Not all north stars work the same way. Before writing P, classify which type your product produces. Each type requires a different E architecture and a different definition of success.

Ongoing
P is delivered in every session. The customer renews because each session is worth having. The relationship is continuous.
Spotify: music finds you before you know you want it — every session.
IndiGo: your flight will leave on time — every flight.
Terminal
P is delivered once. The customer achieves a specific outcome and the relationship transitions — to advocacy, re-acquisition at the next cycle, or referral.
Hinge: "deleted the app" — P is relationship found.
TurboTax: refund in hand before deadline.
Atlys: visa approved before travel.
Intermittent
P recurs at natural intervals determined by external conditions. The customer returns when the condition returns.
Hipcamp: every campsite matches what was described — each trip.
Tax software at filing season.
Terminal P requires a different E architecture. If P is delivered once, retention is not the goal — advocacy and re-acquisition at the next cycle are. Every E investment must serve referral or the next instance of the problem, not session-to-session loyalty.

Three Tests for P

  1. Competitor test: can you replace your P with a competitor's name and it still reads correctly? If yes, it is a category description. Raise it until it fails.
  2. Five-year test: will this outcome still matter to your customer in five years? If the problem ages out, P is at the wrong altitude.
  3. Emotion test: does your customer feel something when they read this P? Relief. Recognition. If it produces no emotional reaction, it is too abstract.

The HOW Audit

Challenge the assumed mechanism in your market before writing P.

  1. Write the assumed HOW. "Every product in this market assumed value = [mechanism]."
  2. Challenge it. "What if value is actually [different mechanism]?"
  3. Ask what if that's wrong. Surface the assumed mechanism and test whether your product also assumes it.

Spotify: every platform assumed "music = something you find." Spotify: "what if music finds you?" This HOW challenge reframes P, O, and E simultaneously.

TurboTax: assumed HOW was "better form-filling UX reduces the chore of tax filing." Ethnographic research revealed that for every 2–4 hours spent filing, users spent equal time collecting data. The bottleneck was not the form — it was the data. Real HOW: move the customer from data entry to data verifier. Automate collection. Let users confirm, not type.

Full Derivation: Notion

Here is the complete journey from HOW Audit to P for a product the target reader almost certainly uses.

StepQuestionAnswer
HOW assumed What mechanism does every workspace tool assume? "A beautiful, flexible interface makes teams more organised." Build great UX. Teams will adopt it. Organisation follows.
HOW challenged What if that's wrong? Beautiful UI is the O, not the mechanism. The real HOW: connected knowledge survives team change. Linkage — not aesthetics — is what retains.
Obstacle thesis Why is P not already true for the target segment? Knowledge is created in silos with no connective tissue. When someone leaves, it leaves with them. The organisation re-learns the same things repeatedly.
P — first draft What is permanently different if Notion works? "Teams that are more organised." Fails competitor test — Confluence can say this too. Too generic.
P — raised More specific. What actually changes? "Teams that never lose institutional knowledge and never repeat the same onboarding from scratch."
Three tests Does it pass competitor, five-year, emotion? Competitor: Confluence cannot claim this for small teams. Pass. Five-year: as long as teams grow and people leave, this matters. Pass. Emotion: every founder who has lost a key hire and watched knowledge walk out the door. Pass.
P is not a tagline. It is a design brief. Every POLE element follows from it. If P had stayed at "more organised," every downstream decision would have been weaker.
"Kingfisher had a tagline. IndiGo had a system. One competed on perception. The other competed on delivery. Only one is still flying."

Chapter Summary

Three P types: Ongoing (every session confirms P), Terminal (P delivered once — requires advocacy architecture), Intermittent (recurs at natural intervals).

P at Feature level cascades: wrong O, wrong L, wrong E.

HOW audit: challenge the assumed mechanism before writing P. TurboTax's insight — data collection was the bottleneck, not form-filling — changed the entire product direction.
Part Two — POLE  |  Chapter 8

O — The Belief Moment

Understandable before first use. Verifiable after first use.

O is the moment a new customer first believes that P is possible. It must fire in session 1. If it does not, the customer leaves before E can begin.

O is not just apparent before use. It breaks the specific sceptical belief the obstacle thesis identified. If you cannot trace O back to the obstacle thesis, your O is not designed — it is a default.

O and the Obstacle Thesis

The obstacle thesis names the specific structural reason the customer has not already solved the problem. O must break exactly the belief that thesis identifies.

ProductObstacle thesisO that breaks it
WiseBanks obscure the real cost, making informed decisions impossible.8x cheaper than your bank. Fee shown before you send. Verifiable before signup.
GrammarlyWriting errors appear too late — after sending — to correct without embarrassment.Red underline in the moment of writing. In Gmail, Docs, Word. Apparent where you write.
CalendlyScheduling requires multiple turns of back-and-forth that degrade credibility.Send one link. The other person picks a time in 30 seconds. Experienced in first use.
Gmail (2004)Email storage forces constant deletion, making the inbox a chore.1 gigabyte free. Verifiable before signup. No trust required. A hard O.

Hard O vs Soft O

  • Hard O: verifiable with a number before first use. "8x cheaper." "1 gigabyte." The number is the proof. No login required.
  • Soft O: experienced in the first 30 seconds before any commitment. Suno: type a description, hear a song. The value is demonstrated, not stated.

Both are valid. Both must fire in session 1. The test: can a prospect grasp it before committing? If you can only advertise it but not verify it, it is E in disguise, not O.

Three O Failure Modes

O fires too late

The belief-breaking moment exists but takes too long to arrive. Day 7 retention is low even when Month 3 is strong. Users explore and leave before O fires.

Fix: move O to session 1. If it cannot get there, redesign the first-session experience around it.
O fires for the wrong segment

O was designed for early adopters but the product is now acquiring a different segment. The belief being broken is not the belief this segment holds.

Fix: name the belief the current acquisition segment holds on arrival. Redesign O to break exactly that belief.
O never fires

No belief-breaking moment exists in session 1. Users explore the product and leave without ever experiencing the moment that would make P feel possible.

Fix: name the specific sceptical belief. Design one session 1 moment that breaks it. Test with five recent sign-ups: "Was there a moment where you thought this might actually work?"

Exercise: Designing O

  1. Write the obstacle thesis: "The primary reason [segment] has not solved [problem] is..."
  2. Name the specific belief the customer holds on arrival.
  3. Describe one session 1 moment that breaks exactly that belief.
  4. Which O failure mode is your product most at risk of: too late, wrong segment, never fires?
  5. Ask five recent sign-ups: "Was there a moment where you thought this might actually work for me?"

Chapter Summary

O breaks a specific belief — the one the obstacle thesis identified. Trace O to the obstacle thesis. If you cannot, it is a default, not a design.

Hard O: verifiable with a number. Soft O: verifiable in first 30 seconds. Both must fire in session 1.

Three failure modes: too late, wrong segment, never fires. Each has a specific fix.

If you can only advertise it — not verify it — it is E in disguise.
Part Two — POLE  |  Chapter 9

L — Liberation

Removes the old barrier. Enables adoption without rebuild.

L is not easy onboarding. It is the deliberate removal of the specific barrier keeping this segment out. Generic ease of use is not L.

Lock-in typeWhat L must doExample
Switching CostImport. Data portability. "Your history comes with you."Spotify imports Apple Music playlists. Taste model begins learning from existing history.
Search CostShow the outcome before commitment. Reverse trial.Warby Parker home try-on. TurboTax shows refund before filing.
InertiaEliminate the first-step requirement. Zero-commitment start."Log one meal. No calorie target required." MFP's current onboarding asks for a goal in session 1 — exactly the wrong design.

How O and L Work Together: Four Relationship Types

O and L often work on the same adoption barrier from different angles. Understanding the relationship type tells you whether your acquisition architecture is as powerful as it can be.

1
Convergence
One feature does both jobs simultaneously. O and L are the same thing. The most powerful acquisition moment.
Gmail 2004: 1GB storage is O (apparent before signup, verifiable by number) and L (broke the reason to stay on existing email — no more deletion pressure) in a single feature.
2
Near-convergence
Different features. Same barrier. Two angles on one wall. Very strong together.
Grammarly: the red underline in Gmail (O — apparent where you write) + works inside every surface without context switch (L — zero new surface required). Both attack the friction of adopting a better writing tool.
3
Sequential
O opens the door. L removes the remaining obstacle in the moment of adoption.
Calendly: seeing the link is O (apparent, effortless to understand). The 30-second first use is L (removes inertia at the exact moment of decision).
4
Independent
O attracts on one axis. L removes a completely unrelated lock-in. Both are necessary but neither supports the other.
Figma: browser collaboration is O (apparent, no install). Free tier with portable component libraries is L (reduces switching cost). Neither overlaps.

Aim for convergence or near-convergence. They are harder to design but produce the strongest acquisition moments.

Chapter Summary

L traces directly to the lock-in type from BELT. Three types produce three completely different liberation designs. Generic ease of use is not L.

Four O+L relationship types: Convergence (one feature, both jobs), Near-convergence (different features, same barrier), Sequential (O then L), Independent (different axes).

Convergence and near-convergence produce the most powerful acquisition moments. Design toward them.
Part Two — POLE  |  Chapter 10

E — The Retention Arc

The architecture that makes leaving increasingly costly

E is not "the retention features." E is the architecture that ensures each session deposits value that did not exist before — until leaving means losing something irreplaceable.

E-Delivered and E-Accrued

E operates in two distinct modes. Understanding which mode you are investing in — and whether E-Delivered is solid before E-Accrued begins — is the most common gap in product roadmaps.

E-Delivered (per session)E-Accrued (across sessions)
What it isValue the product provides in each session. Good recommendations. Fast search. Clean UX. The product performs well.Value that belongs to the user, built inside the product over time. Cannot be transferred. Cannot be replicated by a competitor.
What it drivesDay 1 to Day 30 retention. Week-to-week habit formation.Month 3+ retention and renewal. Real switching cost.
Can a competitor match it?Yes. Within 12 months with investment.No. The competitor cannot replicate what the user has already built.
Switching costWeak alone. User leaves without losing anything they built.Strong. Switching means losing the workspace, history, model — what the user built inside the product.

Three Products, Both Layers

ProductE-Delivered — what each session givesE-Accrued — what the user has builtThe moat test
Stitch Fix Each box has 5 well-chosen pieces. Quality improves with each delivery as the stylist uses your feedback. After 12 months: hundreds of signals — kept, returned, why, size, occasion, budget — build a style profile that took years to develop. Each box is better than the last. Your style profile cannot be transferred. A competitor starts from zero.
Grammarly Real-time underlines, tone suggestions, clarity rewrites. Consistent and useful in every session. A style model that learns your voice — vocabulary, formality, patterns, frequent errors. Month 6 feels nothing like Month 1. Your personal style model cannot be exported. Switching means generic suggestions that feel foreign.
Substack (reader) Each issue is well-written. The reader learns something. The quality of each issue is the reason they opened it. Two years of archives. A relationship with a writer whose thinking you now understand deeply. The hundredth issue lands differently from the first. The archive and relationship are irreplaceable. Switching is not changing the source — it is starting over.
Critical dependency: most teams invest in E-Accrued before E-Delivered is working. The moat forms over a gap that churns customers before they reach it. Fix E-Delivered first. Then layer E-Accrued on a working foundation.

The 5-Step E Design Ladder

1
Decompose P into components
What specific things must be true in each session for P-state to hold? If P is "music finds you before you know you want it," session components include: recommendations feel surprising but right, taste model improves, no effort required.
2
Set the Functional VP floor
Minimum threshold per component. Below this threshold, P-state resets to zero. Spotify: if cross-device sync fails, no amount of Discover Weekly quality saves the session.
3
Derive Experience VP from the obstacle thesis
Which dimension — Speed, Relevance, Clarity, Control — is load-bearing? This is the dimension the obstacle thesis named. It must be the dimension E-Delivered optimises for.
4
Identify the E-Accrued layer
What does the user build inside the product that cannot be taken with them? Spotify: playlists, liked songs, taste model. Notion: workspace, processes, decision log. Figma: component libraries. This is the moat.
5
E-P Alignment — Cut features that don't trace to P
For every feature: does it trace to Step 1 via the obstacle thesis in two steps or fewer? If not, cut it. It is consuming roadmap without advancing P-state.

The Referral Moment

Referral is not a feature. It is the moment E-Accrued becomes perceivable and shareable. Spotify Wrapped works because it fires in December — after 12 months of E-Accrued listening history. The value was already there. Wrapped made it visible. Triggering referral at adoption (Stage 3) before E-Accrued has formed produces an empty chart and no sharing.

The rule: referral fires when P is realised and E-Accrued is perceivable. Not before.

Exercise: E Design — Run the Ladder

  1. Decompose your P into 3–5 components. What must be true in each session?
  2. Set the floor for each component.
  3. Name the load-bearing Experience VP dimension. Which one addresses the obstacle thesis?
  4. Identify the E-Accrued layer. What does the user build that they cannot export?
  5. Apply Step 5 to your three highest-engagement features. Can each be traced to Step 1 in two steps?

Chapter Summary

E-Delivered retains week to week. A competitor can match it. E-Accrued creates real switching cost — leaving means losing what was built.

5-step ladder: decompose P, set floor, derive Experience VP from obstacle thesis, identify E-Accrued layer, cut features that cannot trace to Step 1.

Non-obvious truth: most PMs optimise the acquisition layer while the retention layer collapses underneath them. Strong O + weak E is a leaky bucket. Fix E-Delivered first. Then E-Accrued. Then referral.
Part Three — Value Currency  |  Chapter 11

Value Currency

Time, Data, Money

Every product accumulates at least one currency. The moat only forms when the customer can feel what they have built. If the moat is invisible, it does not protect.

T
Time — The Seed
Sessions invested. Weakest alone — a competitor can buy it back with a discount. Only becomes load-bearing when it enables Data to form.

Spotify streams. Strava logs. Duolingo days practiced.
D
Data — The Moat
Irreplaceable personalisation built from the customer's own behaviour. Cannot be transferred. The competitor cannot replicate it without the customer's entire history.

Spotify taste model. Notion knowledge graph. Superhuman triage patterns.
M
Money — The Lock
Activates last. The customer pays to protect what has been built — the data asset they cannot rebuild elsewhere. Not a paywall; protection for the moat they have created.

Spotify Premium. Notion team plan. LinkedIn Recruiter.

The Sequence Varies by Product

Most products progress Time → Data → Money. But the starting currency depends on what the customer deposits first.

ProductStarting currencySequence

Currency in Research: Confirmatory vs Commitment Yes

Value currency applies beyond product retention. In customer research, each escalation in ask reveals the difference between a polite yes and a real commitment. The gap tells you whether the pain is real.

RoundCurrency askedSignal levelWhat you learn
"Are you interested in a carpooling app that gives you extra income and good company?"Email address — TimeConfirmatory yesConfirms the market cares. Does not confirm they will change behaviour.

Chapter Summary

Three currencies: Time (seed), Data (moat), Money (lock). Direction always toward the moat.

Sequence is product-specific. The moat only protects when the customer can feel it. Invisible moats do not retain.

Three failure modes: Stuck at Time (competitors can buy it back), Data Invisible (moat exists but customer cannot feel it), Premature Money (capture before Data is established).

In research: confirmatory yes costs nothing. Commitment yes costs identity. The gap reveals whether the pain is real.
Part Four — Value Lifecycle  |  Chapter 12

The Value Lifecycle

Nine stages. Three actors. Design for all of them.

Value does not stop at delivery. It must be promised, perceived, delivered, received, realised, reinforced, and captured to complete the cycle. Most products design for Stages 1–4 and leave everything else to chance.

Three Actors

ActorWhoOwnsFailure mode
Value GeneratorThe company. Creates, promises, delivers, reinforces, and captures value.Stages 1, 2, 4, 8 (shared), 9Asks for capture before realisation. Paywall before moat.
Value DistributorThe channel. Not always a person — often the product itself. Wrapped. The weekly email. The push notification.Stage 5. Co-owns Stage 8.Distributes at wrong moment or wrong channel. Referral before perception.
Value ReceiverPerceives, experiences, realises, and responds. The referral loop begins here.Stages 3, 6, 7.Receives value but cannot articulate what they got. Moat is invisible.

The Nine Stages

Stage 8: The Highest-Leverage Gap

Stage 8 is Reinforcement: making what the customer has built visible before they consider leaving. It is the highest-leverage gap in most product roadmaps because:

  • The moat exists at Stage 7 (Realisation). But if the customer cannot see it, it does not protect.
  • Without Reinforcement, the customer evaluates alternatives without knowing what they would lose.
  • Most products design Stages 1–7 deliberately. Stage 8 happens by default or not at all.

Spotify Wrapped fires in December — not in January when users sign up.

By December, users have 12 months of E-Accrued listening history. Wrapped makes that history visible and shareable. That perception (Stage 3) triggered referral (feeding back into Stage 5). The moat was already there at Stage 7. Wrapped made it perceivable. Triggering Wrapped in January would have produced an empty chart and no sharing.

Stage 8 is not a feature. It is the Distributor's job at the right moment.

Stage 2 is not one promise. Each POLE element makes a different promise at a different stage, evaluated at a different moment.

Exercise: Lifecycle Audit

  1. For each of the nine stages: write one sentence describing what your product does there.
  2. Mark each: D (deliberately designed) or ? (happened by default).
  3. Count your D marks. Most products score 3–4 out of 9.
  4. Stage 8 specifically: what is the moment and channel where E-Accrued is made visible before the customer considers leaving?

Chapter Summary

Nine stages. Three actors. Each stage has a primary owner.

Stage 8 (Reinforcement) is the highest-leverage gap in most roadmaps. Making E-Accrued visible before the customer considers leaving. Most products design Stages 1–7 deliberately. Stage 8 is left to chance.

Value Promise (Stage 2) is four promises — P, O, L, E — each made at a different moment and evaluated at a different stage.

Capture (Stage 9) must follow Realisation (Stage 7). Capture before Realisation is a paywall. It extracts value the customer has not yet felt.
Part Five — Diagnostics  |  Chapter 13

The Weakest Link Audit

One weak element caps everything else

POLE elements compound each other. A strong E cannot compensate for a weak P. The weakest link determines the ceiling of the entire product.

The Audit

Score each POLE element on two dimensions. Clarity: can a customer state this element clearly without prompting? Credibility: do customers believe it based on actual experience?

ScoreWhat it means
High (H)Most customers can state it clearly and believe it from experience.
Medium (M)Some customers can state it. Credibility is partial or inconsistent.
Low (L)Most customers cannot articulate it, or do not believe it from experience.

The element with the lowest combined score is the Weakest Link. It is your only priority. Never fix H elements when an L exists.

Fix Sequence: P First. Always.

  • P is L: fix P before anything else. O cannot fire for a clear belief when P is undefined. L cannot remove the right barrier. E has nothing to compound.
  • O is L: fix after P is confirmed.
  • L is L: fix after O is confirmed.
  • E is L: fix last. E failure is almost always a symptom of weak P, not a root cause.

LinkedIn: A Worked Example

900 million users. The Weakest Link is not where most product teams would look.

ElementScoreDiagnosis
PL"Professional networking." No specific outcome for any specific segment. Cannot complete "I use LinkedIn because..." in outcome terms.
OLSession 1: scroll feed, see strangers' content. No moment where a career goal feels closer. O cannot fire because P is undefined.
LHProfessional network was already on LinkedIn. Adoption required zero rebuild. L is High — but inherited from network effects, not designed.
EMFeed, messaging, content exist. E-Accrued is weak: no Data currency beyond connection count. No personalisation moat.
The LinkedIn lesson: L is High (network lock-in is genuine) but L without P means the product retains without convincing. That is a ceiling, not a foundation.

Fix: write P at a specific segment level. "For professionals actively seeking their next role, LinkedIn delivers..." Everything else follows from one sentence.

Exercise: Weakest Link Audit

  1. Score each POLE element H/M/L on Clarity and Credibility.
  2. Identify the Weakest Link.
  3. Write one specific change that would move that element from L to M.
  4. Estimate: what would the retention impact be if this fix worked?

Chapter Summary

Score each POLE element H/M/L on Clarity and Credibility. Lowest score = Weakest Link.

Fix order: P first, then O, then L, then E.

L is High without P is a ceiling, not a foundation (LinkedIn). E failure is almost always a symptom of weak P, not a root cause.
Part Six — Applied  |  Chapter 14

Six Case Studies

Good. Can be great.

Six real products. The framework applied in full. Each case surfaces something the product's own team almost certainly knows is a gap — and shows what a targeted fix would produce.

MyFitnessPal

P at feature level. The insight layer was always there. Nobody built it.

BELTFinding
BAdults log food obsessively in Notes apps, paper notebooks. Workaround is elaborate and self-sustaining.
ETo understand how food choices affect how they feel — not to hit a calorie number.
LActivation Inertia. Barrier is belief: "I am not the kind of person who sticks to this."
TEnabling. Each logged meal contributes to a personal data model.

Loom

O fires for the sender. It never fires for the recipient. The real barrier is on the receiving end.

Weakest Link: O — for the wrong actor. O exists and fires for the sender. But until the recipient's O fires ("this 90-second video replaced a meeting that would have taken 47 minutes to schedule"), async culture does not spread. Loom optimised the wrong side of the interaction.

Fix: redesign O for the recipient. Build Stage 8 at team level: "Your team sent 43 Looms this month. 24 meetings replaced." Makes the cultural shift visible and irreversible.

Strava

The moat is enormous. The customer cannot see it. Stage 8 does not exist.

The situationThe gap
4 years of activity data. Personal records on 67 segments. A running community of 340 followers. T is strongly Enabling.The product never shows "4,200km since 2019." Users consider switching without realising what they would give up. Data Invisible failure mode.
Felt switching cost: near-zero. Actual switching cost: very high.Stage 8 (Reinforcement) does not exist. Designed for delivery. Neglected reinforcement entirely.

Fix: Build Stage 8 — Annual Year in Sport: "4,200km. 67 PRs. 12 races. Your pace improved 18% since 2021." Visible before renewal and before alternatives appear. Surface the moat before the customer considers leaving.

Robinhood

The obstacle thesis was wrong. Every feature built on it was wrong — regardless of execution quality.

The founding obstacle thesis: "The barrier is commissions." Actual barrier: financial confidence. Every gamification feature was directionally correct for "make more trades" and directionally wrong for "build confidence." The wrong thesis is not a small mistake — it is the architecture.

Fix: correct the thesis before redesigning a single feature. "The barrier is not commissions. It is the fear of making the wrong decision." Redesign O to celebrate the decision, not the execution. Redesign E to build confidence over time, not trade frequency.

Rippling

Strong product. P at Feature level. The compounding E layer is never shown.

CurrentShould be
P: "HR and payroll in one place." Describes the tool category.P: "For fast-growing companies, Rippling delivers one employee record that flows to every system — so operations overhead stops compounding with headcount."
Stage 8 absent. The moat never surfaced to customers."You have 14 systems connected around 230 employee records. Switching means rebuilding each integration." Surface the moat before renewal.

GitHub Copilot

O fires immediately. The moat never forms. Transient when it should be Enabling.

T is currently Transient. Every session starts from roughly the same baseline. The product does not learn this developer's patterns deeply enough for the experience to compound. Month 6 and Month 1 feel similar. The moat that should exist has not been designed.

The Copilot lesson: strong O is not sufficient for a moat. The moat requires E-Accrued: the product must learn this developer specifically. Without personalisation, Copilot is an excellent transient product competing on features — not an enabling product competing on irreplaceability.

Fix: design the personalisation layer. Track per-developer: which suggestions are accepted, which patterns repeat. Build a developer fingerprint. Make Month 6 noticeably better than Month 1. Build Stage 8: "Copilot handled 2,400 lines of boilerplate this month. Your acceptance rate is 71%, up from 49% in month 1. It is learning your style."

Application Guide

Run the Diagnostic in 30 Minutes

Five steps. One output. Applied to any product you know.

Every concept in this book points here. The framework is only valuable when it is in contact with a real product. This guide takes you from blank page to diagnosis in 30 minutes.

1
B — Find the behaviour (5 min)
Write down the specific workaround your most committed early customers used before your product existed. Make it concrete. "They used spreadsheets" is not specific enough. If you cannot name a specific workaround: stop here. You have a hypothesis, not confirmed demand.
2
E — Name the life change (5 min)
Complete: "For [specific segment], [product] delivers [specific outcome], so [life is different in this specific way]." Run the So What? technique: keep asking until the answer stops mentioning the product. Then answer: is this problem enduring or transient?
3
L — Identify the lock-in type (5 min)
Which one describes your segment: Switching Cost, Search Cost, or Inertia? Then look at your current onboarding. Write one sentence describing what it does to address this specific barrier. If the sentence describes generic setup rather than the specific lock-in type you named: your L is misdiagnosed.
4
T — Validate the type (5 min)
If a customer stopped using your product for six months and came back, would the product know them? Would the 601st session feel different from the first? If yes: Enabling — build the E architecture. If no: Transient — do not invest in personalisation. Design for P-Salience instead.
5
Score your POLE (10 min)
Score each element H/M/L on Clarity and Credibility. The lowest combined score is your Weakest Link. That is your only priority. Fix it before investing in any other element.
